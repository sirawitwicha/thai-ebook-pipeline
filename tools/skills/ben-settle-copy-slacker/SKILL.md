# Ben Settle Copy Slacker — Complete Methodology

The FULL 5-part sales letter formula from Ben Settle's *Copy Slacker* book, sourced directly from the original text (Chapters 2-8). This is NOT a summary or adaptation — it captures the actual nuance, examples, and psychology from the book.

## Core Philosophy

**"It's not about the weedkiller. It's about the crab grass."**

Research the MARKET, not the product. When you know the market well enough, you can write most of the sales copy without even seeing the product. Old school copywriters (Gene Schwartz, Milt Pierce) wrote ads completely BLIND — they would pretend they had God-like powers to grant the market whatever they wanted, write the ad promising those benefits, then create the product to fulfill as many as possible, removing claims they couldn't deliver.

**"Your headline is nothing more than your best bullet elevated."**

Bullets are the FOUNDATION of all copy. Gary Halbert wrote ads that were almost ALL bullets. The whole point of the beginning of the ad was to get to the bullets as fast as possible.

**"You have no client but the market."** — Gene Schwartz

## Part 1: Criminally Profitable Research (Chapter 2)

### The Core Insight

"It's not about the weedkiller. It's about the crab grass." Your job is to crawl inside the market's psychology. Not what they SAY they want in surveys — but what they actually BUY, SAY, and DO in public forums, comment sections, and competitor reviews.

### The 45 Questions

These are the questions Ben makes $15K consulting clients fill out. Answer as many as possible through observation (not surveys):

**Pain & Fear (Q1-8):**
1. What keeps them awake at night? (Sunday night insomnia, staring at ceiling)
2. What are they terrified of? (List in order, including things too ashamed to admit)
3. What causes them pain? (Physical, emotional, psychological, spiritual)
4. What are their insecurities? (Things they hope nobody finds out)
5. What makes them angry? (Real or imagined injustices)
6. Who exactly are they angry at? (Boss, ex-spouse, government, gurus who screwed them)
7. Who are they blaming for their problems? (Society blames everyone else — use this)
8. What do they worry about most? (Not just afraid — UTTERLY TERRIFIED of)

**Identity & Values (Q9-14):**
9. What are their values? (NEVER project your values onto them)
10. What religion are they? (Affects worldview, language, taboos)
11. What are their secret fantasies? (Things they'd never admit in public)
12. What do they want most? (Not "need" — WANT)
13. What income bracket? (Determines price anchoring)
14. What's their education level? (Determines reading level)

**The Enemy (Q15-20):**
15. What common enemies unite them? (VITAL — shared enemies = tribal bond)
16. Who screwed them over? (Gurus, banks, ex-business partners)
17. What false beliefs do they hold? (Things they believe that aren't true)
18. What solutions have they tried that failed? (Crucial for credibility)
19. What's the "bar complaint" — what do they complain about to friends over drinks?
20. What are they addicted to? (Not just substances — dopamine from buying, etc.)

**Language & Media (Q21-30):**
21. What slang/vernacular do they use? (Write like an insider, not an outsider)
22. What TV shows/movies do they watch?
23. What books/magazines do they read?
24. What websites/forums do they visit? (Where do they hang out online?)
25. What conferences/events do they attend?
26. What gurus/authorities do they follow?
27. What phrases trigger them emotionally?
28. What is the #1 urgent problem they're trying to solve RIGHT NOW?
29. What do they fear losing most? (Status, health, family, money, identity)
30. What do they secretly believe about themselves? (Insecurities = gold)

**Competitive Intelligence (Q31-45):**
31. Who are your competitors? (Not just direct — anyone solving same problem)
32. What claims do competitors make?
33. What proof do competitors use?
34. What are competitors' weaknesses?
35. What are competitors' strengths?
36. What do competitor reviews say? (Especially 1-3 star)
37. What competitor ads are running? (Facebook Ad Library, swipe files)
38. What bonuses do competitors offer?
39. What guarantees do competitors use?
40. What price points dominate the market?
41. What themes/emotions do competitors use?
42. What's missing from competitor copy? (THE GAP — your advantage)
43. What proof elements can YOU claim that NO competitor can?
44. What media covers this niche?
45. What's the "Golf God" phrase — something a prospect said that COULD be a headline?

### The Research File System

After collecting all data, create one master "chaos file" with these sections:
- **Headline ideas** — anything that could be a headline
- **Opening paragraph ideas** — even just fragments
- **Story material** — anecdotes, examples, case studies
- **Bullet material** — claims, benefits, pain points, proof elements
- **Close material** — objections, guarantee angles, bonus ideas, PS material
- **Proof elements** — facts, case studies, testimonials, credentials, statistics

## Part 2: Headlines (Chapter 3)

### The Shotgun Rack

*"What can you say in 6 or 7 words when the person opens the door and, instead of slamming it, they grab you by the arm, yank you inside, and demand you tell them more?"*

Find this FIRST — before writing any headline. It's the core emotional payload.

### The 11 Headline Types

**1. Proof Headline**
Join proof to your promise. "One fully believed claim is more persuasive than 1,000 half-believed claims." — Gary Bencivenga. Proof is the RAREST element in marketing. Like gold. Put named authorities, specific numbers, or documented results in the headline.

*Example:* "World's leading military combat instructor declassifies the secret supplement used by the Navy SEALS to battle pain, injury, body fat, and cardiovascular health problems."

**2. Market-Oriented Headline**
Speak to the market's pain, fear, or insecurity WITHOUT any claim or benefit. Pure shotgun rack. Requires tight research.

*Example:* "Warning: Do not read this if you have moral, ethical, or religious reasons against hurting or even killing someone who violently attacks you, your wife, or your kids."

**3. Emotion Headline**
Tap into the dominant resident emotion (rage, exhaustion, defiance, betrayal). One word can be enough.

**4. Blatant Offer Headline**
State the most irresistible offer right in the headline. No tease. Pure offer.

**5. Contrast Headline**
Two ideas that don't belong together. Contrast "clamps down hard on a person's psychology and doesn't let go." Humans need consistency — contrast disturbs that.

*Example:* "The incredible copywriting secret (used by both P.T. Barnum and Mother Theresa)"

**6. Tabloid Headline**
Sensational, dramatic, "can't look away." Study National Enquirer, Cosmo, Men's Health covers.

**7. Curiosity Headline**
Creates an itch they MUST scratch. Fascinating but incomplete. "Even Facebook click-bait works because curiosity creates chaos in someone's brain."

**8. Straight Benefit Headline**
Sometimes you don't need a trick. The benefit is urgent and specific enough.

**9. Question Headline**
Adding a question mark tones things down, removes hype. "Do you know..." variation works well.

**10. Objection Headline**
Name their skepticism right in the headline. Disarms the inner critic.

**11. Combine & Conquer**
Mix 2+ types. Proof + Curiosity. Emotion + Market-Oriented. The most powerful headlines often combine.

### The Knock-Knocks Exercise
5-7 headlines that are 6-7 words maximum. Drawn from exact prospect phrases. Instant recognition — "that's ME" within 1 second.

## Part 3: Opening Paragraphs (Chapter 4)

### The 9 Openers

**1. "Let me tell you a story"**
Old faithful. Crystal clear. Gets to the point. People like stories.

**2. If/Then**
Gary Halbert's favorite. The word "if" qualifies any claim, removes the "bull shit factor." Built-in belief.

**3. If/Then (with Proof)**
Add proof to the If/Then. Addresses the skeptics — which is 2-5x bigger than hyper buyers. "If you'd like to know a secret way to make yourself near invincible in a fight, then here's how ancient gladiators and samurai did it centuries ago."

**4. Brutal Honesty**
"Most books lie to you. This one won't." Disarms through transparency.

**5. Secret/Forbidden Knowledge**
"Something they don't want you to know." Creates instant curiosity.

**6. Warning/Threat**
"Don't read this unless..." Qualifies the reader while making them want it more.

**7. "Do you know?"**
Variation on question. Invites self-diagnosis.

**8. Bizarre/Weird Story Hook**
"Let me tell you a bizarre story about..." Pattern interrupt.

**9. Tabloid Scandal**
Opens like a news exposé. "What [authority] doesn't want you to know."

### Market Breakdown (Ken McCarthy)

- 50% — Will never buy from direct response, no matter what
- 5% — Hyper buyers who buy everything (refund most, complain most)
- 45% — SKEPTICS. Want what you have but need to be SOLD. This is where you make your money.

## Part 4: Stories (Chapter 5)

### Why Stories

Top Gun never said "join the Navy" — but recruitment soared so high the military put recruiters IN the theaters. Stories sell WITHOUT asking to buy. Ray-Ban sales increased 30% and the brand wasn't even shown.

"The brain craves to be sold with stories."

### The 5 Story Types

**Type 1: Story About You (Origin Story)**
Your origin story — how you discovered the solution. Hollywood's most profitable kind of movie. "What's my origin story?" — write it down. Can be used for decades.

**Type 2: Story About Someone Else**
A composite from real research. Take real pain points and dramatize them through a character.

**Type 3: Parable/Dramatized Story**
When you have composite pain data but no specific person. Create a story that represents the market's experience.

**Type 4: Historical Story**
Use documented history with dates, names, events. Proof-laden. Authority-building.

**Type 5: Meet The Guru**
Named authority with documented credentials. Shows the source of the knowledge.

### Story Rules

1. **"Short enough to get attention, long enough to cover the details"** — like a woman's skirt
2. **Tension is king** — Heaven and Hell effect. Show them in hell (the problem), ascend to heaven (the solution)
3. **Thin the plot, don't thicken it** — get to the point fast
4. **"Nothing bad ever happens to a writer"** — everything is story material. Especially bad things.

## Part 5: Bullets (Chapter 6)

### Why Bullets Are Everything

"Bullet writing is the most important 'writing' part of sales copy." One bullet buried deep in an ad can make the entire sale. Gary Halbert's *Entrepreneur* magazine ad had a bullet that got people calling a FULL YEAR after it ran.

"Your headline is nothing more than a version of your best bullet."

### The 12 Bullet Types

**Type 1: Curiosity**
Creates an itch they HAVE to scratch. Find what they're interested in, tell them something fascinating but INCOMPLETE. The incompleteness IS the hook. Creates chaos in their brain.

*"Seventeen lies all intelligent women always tell men!" — Gary Halbert*
*"Bills it's okay to pay late" — Mel Martin*

**Type 2: "Can't Be Done"**
A claim right at the cusp of reality — maybe a foot on the other side. Not impossible, but ALMOST. Forces people to want to respond just to get the answer.

*"How to vacuum, dust, and clean your dresser drawer without removing its contents!" — Gene Schwartz*

**Type 3: Proof and Credibility**
Attach a named authority, historical figure, or documented result. Each proof element is a link in a chain. "One fully believed claim is more persuasive than 1,000 half-believed claims." Stifles the prospect's inner skeptic chanting "that's bull crap!"

*"A scientifically proven way (discovered by Isaac Newton) to hit another man with 'sledge hammer' like force."*

**Type 4: Contrast**
Two ideas that don't belong together. Psychologically disturbing — humans need consistency, contrast disturbs that. They must resolve the contradiction.

*"The incredible copywriting secret (used by both P.T. Barnum and Mother Theresa)"*
*"A popular 'vegetable' that can make you fat as a whale"*

**Type 5: Pain (Physical or Psychological)**
Pain sticks in the brain better than ANYTHING. Memory trainers use pain imagery. Physical: "pissing blood." Psychological: "disinherit their children." People can't resist reading about pain they're going through.

*"A secret 'bear hug' defense that's so ruthless, there's a good chance he'll be pissing blood immediately afterwards."*
*"What many people do in their wills that could disinherit their children." — David Deutsch*

**Type 6: Straight Benefit**
State the benefit directly. No bells, no whistles. Optional: "double whammy" (John Carlton) — claim the benefit, then expand on it in the same bullet.

*"How to hit the golf ball's sweet spot every time you swing the club. (Hit the ball in this exact spot and you almost can't help driving the ball farther and straighter down the fairway.)"*

**Type 7: Tell Them What NOT To Do**
Inherently interesting — especially if they're doing it. Use hyper-specifics: "four words," "the #1 WORST mistake."

*"Four words that will destroy your chances of sponsoring new reps — even the ones who already want to sign up!"*

**Type 8: The Giveaway**
Give away the ENTIRE secret. No tease. Full disclosure. Proves you're not a charlatan. Makes all other, more outrageous bullets believable. Use sparingly — once or twice per list.

*"Nausea — settle a queasy stomach with a bowl of milk toast. Amazing!"*

**Type 9: The 90% Solution**
Give 90% of the secret, withhold the crucial 10%. Like baiting a fishing hook — just enough to get the bite, not the whole can of worms. David Deutsch's method.

*"Why you should never chew gum if you are trying to lose weight!"*

**Type 10: Dramatic Sub-Headline**
After 40-50 bullets, the list becomes monotonous. Turn the last 6-8 words of a strong bullet into a bold, centered sub-headline. PATTERN INTERRUPT. Use every 15-20 bullets.

**Type 11: Compressed Finale**
Like fireworks grand finale. Lead-in bullet ("And a whole lot more, including...") → paragraph of 10-15 short bullets separated by "..." → end with dramatic sub-head. Density creates almost hypnotic effect.

**Type 12: Expansion Bullets**
Pick a bullet. "Let's take a second to unpack this." Expand 2-4 sentences with editorial feel. Creates the "loop effect" — expansion → bullets → expansion → bullets. Draws reader deeper. Use every 10-15 bullets.

### Bullet Layout Tactics

1. **Beef up bookends** — strongest bullet FIRST, strongest bullet LAST. Skimmers read these two first.
2. **Loop effect** — expansion every 10-15 bullets. Almost hypnotic.
3. **Vary up ammo** — no two adjacent bullets the same type. Like Wing Chun: keep them psychologically off balance.
4. **Eye relief** — alternate bold/not bold. Simple 3-second design tip.

### How to Get Better

Paul Hartunian's method: write EVERYTHING in bullet form — even phone messages. Study the best: David Deutsch, Gary Bencivenga, Gary Halbert, Mel Martin, John Carlton, Parris Lampropoulos. Hand-copy their bullets daily.

## Part 6: Closing (Chapter 7)

### The 4 Close Components

**Component 1: The Guarantee**
- You don't ALWAYS need one (Ben doesn't use them on his own products — prefers "burn the boats" committed buyers)
- Mass market cold prospects: USE a guarantee. Be as dramatic and generous as possible.
- Lifetime > 30-day. "Your grandkids can return it!" — John Carlton
- Dramatic: "I'll TRIPLE your money back, you can keep the product, AND I'll pay $100 cash out of my pocket!"
- NEVER do refund-plus-cash unless you can handle the refunds

**Component 2: Bonuses**
- Pile them on. People should buy JUST for the bonuses.
- Each bonus must be so compelling it could be sold standalone.
- Name bonuses using the market's slang/vernacular.

**Component 3: The Takeaway**
- NOT a tactic — an ETHIC. Must be genuine, rooted in honesty.
- "Dials up their curiosity to 11" — when told they can't have something, they want it more.
- Must be matter-of-fact, not performative attitude. Smart customers smell manipulation.
- Can be more powerful than guarantee + bonuses COMBINED when done right.

**Component 4: The Almighty PS**
- Second most-read part of an ad.
- Testimonials go in PS or sidebars — NEVER interrupt narrative flow ("like putting up a sign that says STOP READING").
- Types: deadline PS, story PS, curiosity PS, testimonial PS.

### The Offer Block Structure
Connector → What They Get → Price → Guarantee → Deadline → CTA → PS

## Part 7: Assembly — Putting It All Together (Chapter 8)

### The Process

1. **Research dump** — put all research into one chaos file, organized by section (headline ideas, opener ideas, story material, bullet material, close material, proof elements)

2. **The Money Message Massage** — massage everything together. Take one section at a time. Whatever interests you, play with it. Reword, add, subtract, delete.

3. **Gene Schwartz 33:33 Timer Method** — Set kitchen timer for 33:33. You're not allowed to do anything except look at the manuscript. No internet, no phone. Get bored. Something WILL stand out. Start there. Aim for 4-6 sessions per day.

4. **"Write Drunk"** (Hemingway) — First draft: write without inhibition. No blockages. Door closed. Nobody is judging it. Just you, the market, and the words.

5. **"Edit Sober"** — Ruthless editing. Ask: Is this clear? Fewer words? Dramatic enough? Does this paragraph earn its place? Will the market care? ONLY THE MARKET'S OPINION MATTERS.

6. **Read it out loud 10 times** — Each pass has ONE job. Don't multitask across passes.

### Editing Checklist
- Is every claim traceable to research data?
- Have I used the market's exact language from the slang dictionary?
- Is proof attached to every major claim?
- Are bullets varied in type? (No two adjacent same type)
- Is there a clear Heaven & Hell arc in the story?
- Is the takeaway genuine, not performative?
- Does the PS have a clear purpose?
- Have I read it out loud?
