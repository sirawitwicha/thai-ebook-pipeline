Sarah did everything right. Good grades. Good school. Good job at a Fortune 500 company where she genuinely believed the deal was simple — you work hard, you do great work, you get ahead.

For the first couple years, it seemed to be working. She got solid performance reviews. Her projects delivered. Her colleagues seemed to respect her.

But there was something happening in that office she couldn't quite see. Something nobody had ever told her to look for.

---

It started small. A suggestion she made in a one-on-one with her boss showed up in the next all-hands meeting — presented as his idea. Word for word. She told herself it was a coincidence.

Then it happened again. And again.

Then she noticed that a coworker who seemed friendly — always asking about her weekend, always commiserating about the boss — was somehow always in the room when decisions about her projects were being made. And somehow, those projects kept getting reassigned. To him.

But you know what? That wasn't even the worst part.

---

The worst part was the performance review.

"Sarah, you're doing great work. But I'm getting feedback that you need more visibility."

Visibility. She'd later learn what that word really meant — you haven't played the game.

She sat in her car in the parking garage after that meeting and cried. Not because of the review. Because she realized she had no idea what "visibility" even meant. Nobody had taught her the rules. And the people winning clearly knew them.

---

That night, unable to sleep, she started searching. "Why do incompetent people get promoted." "How to deal with a boss who steals credit." "Office politics tactics."

She fell into a rabbit hole. A YouTube video here. A Reddit thread there. An article about something called "the 12 psychological manipulation techniques your coworkers are using on you right now."

She read through the list — and felt her stomach drop.

Gaslighting. Credit theft. Baiting. Projection. Quiet firing.

Every single one was happening in her office. She just hadn't had names for them.

That was the moment everything changed.

---

She didn't confront anyone. She didn't quit. She didn't even change her work.

She just started watching. And within a week, she could see the patterns everywhere. The credit thief's exact moves. The gossip's flying monkeys. The boss's gaslighting — making people doubt their own perception so he could control the narrative.

And then, in a meeting, it happened. Her boss started to claim her idea.

But this time she was ready. She asked one question — the one that forces a manipulator to be specific — and watched him completely unravel.

She was no longer prey.
