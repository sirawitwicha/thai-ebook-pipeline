# The 10 Editing Passes — Detailed Checklists

Adapted from Ben Settle's "read it out loud 10 times" methodology from Chapter 8. Each pass has ONE job. Do not multitask across passes — that defeats the purpose.

**Why 10 passes?** "I've done it less than 10 times, and the copy always suffered when I did. There is something about 10 times that just makes it all work." — Ben Settle

**Pacing**: Do 3-4 passes per session. Between sessions, step away — let the draft "sit." Budget 2-3 sessions total. In AI terms, each pass is a separate editing run with a specific lens.

---

## Pass 1: Market Language Check

**Check against**: `slang-dictionary.md` (all categories), `copy-brief.md` → Bar Complaint Monologue

### Checklist

- [ ] Read every sentence. Is this how a real person in this market talks?
- [ ] Cross-check 10 random sentences against the slang dictionary. Do they use the market's EXACT words?
- [ ] Flag any "corporate speak" — sanitized, HR-friendly, or academic language
- [ ] Flag any "copywriter speak" — language that sounds like marketing, not conversation
- [ ] Replace flagged language with verbatim phrases from the slang dictionary Direct Quotes section
- [ ] Read the Bar Complaint Monologue aloud. Does the letter sound like this person wrote it?

### Common failures
- Using "individuals" instead of "people"
- Using "utilize" instead of "use"
- Writing "experience heightened workplace satisfaction" instead of "actually like going to work on Monday"
- Sanitized descriptions of pain that real people describe in raw, profane, or vivid language

---

## Pass 2: Continuity Check

**Check against**: All output files — the narrative chain must be unbroken.

### Checklist

- [ ] **Headline → Opener**: Same emotional register? Same enemy (implicit or explicit)?
- [ ] **Opener → Story**: Same protagonist? Same voice (1st/3rd person)? Same situation?
- [ ] **Story → Bullets**: Same pain points? Same transformation? Same specific details?
- [ ] **Bullets → Close**: Same desire language? Same proof elements? Same emotional payoff?
- [ ] **Voice consistency**: 1st person throughout or 3rd person throughout? No switching mid-letter.
- [ ] **Enemy consistency**: The enemy mentioned in the headline is the same enemy in the story, bullets, and close.
- [ ] **Pronoun check**: "I" vs "we" vs "they" — pick one and stick. "You" for the reader — always.

### Common failures
- Headline says "they're playing a game" but story never mentions a game
- Opener is 1st person ("I am not a...") but story switches to 3rd person ("David was...")
- Different enemy names across sections: "credit thief" in bullets but "office politician" in close
- Voice shift from raw/candid to formal/polished mid-letter

---

## Pass 3: Tension Check

**No data file needed.** This is a pure reading-flow check.

### Checklist

- [ ] **Section 1 → 2**: Does the opener end with a hook that makes you NEED the story?
- [ ] **Section 2 → 3**: Does each story section (Setup → Escalates → Rock Bottom → Discovery → Transformation) end with a reason to keep reading?
- [ ] **Section 3 → 4**: Do the bullets maintain the tension the story built? Or does energy drop?
- [ ] **Section 4 → 5**: Does the close maintain tension from the last bullet? Or does it feel like a hard pivot into "sales mode"?
- [ ] **Heaven & Hell arc**: Can you trace the full emotional journey — Hell (section 1-2) → Descent (section 3) → Turning Point (section 4) → Heaven (section 5)?
- [ ] **No early release**: Does any section accidentally resolve tension too early? (e.g., revealing the solution before the pain is fully felt)
- [ ] **"But you know what? That wasn't even the worst part"** — Does the story have this beat? Does the emotional momentum build or plateau?

### Common failures
- Story resolves too quickly — Rock Bottom is a paragraph instead of a descent
- Bullets start strong then become a list
- Close feels like a separate document appended to the end
- The bridge from story into bullets releases all tension ("and that's how he solved it" — no, that should come LATER)

---

## Pass 4: Specificity Check

**Check against**: All research files. Every claim needs a specific anchor.

### Checklist

- [ ] Scan for **vague quantifiers**: "many," "some," "a lot," "numerous," "various" → replace with specific numbers
- [ ] Scan for **unnamed authorities**: "studies show," "experts say," "research proves" → name the study, expert, or research
- [ ] Scan for **generic timeframes**: "soon," "quickly," "eventually," "after a while" → replace with specific timelines
- [ ] Scan for **anonymous people**: "one person," "a client," "someone" → use names (even composites like "David")
- [ ] Scan for **vague places**: "at work," "in an office," "somewhere" → be specific
- [ ] Verify every proof claim has a source (named publication, year, researcher, or statistic)

### Replacements

| Vague | Specific (from research data) |
|---|---|
| "Many employees" | "567 employees across 3 industries in a peer-reviewed study" |
| "A long time ago" | "In 1532, Machiavelli wrote..." |
| "Studies show" | "The American Psychological Association documented..." |
| "People hate office politics" | "'Most days I don't feel like going to work' — verbatim from a 34-year-old engineer" |
| "It works fast" | "One reader spotted a manipulation attempt within 10 seconds of opening the field guide" |

## Pass 5: "Would They Care?" Check

**Check against**: `structured-profile.json` → pain_and_fear, `copy-brief.md` → Primary Hook

### Checklist

- [ ] Read every sentence. Ask: "Would someone in this market care about this?"
- [ ] Flag any sentence that's about YOU (the writer), not THEM (the reader)
- [ ] Flag any sentence that's "showing off" — vocabulary, cleverness, wit-for-wit's-sake
- [ ] Flag any sentence that could be cut without losing anything the market cares about
- [ ] Cut everything flagged. "Kill your darlings" — Stephen King.
- [ ] After cutting, re-read. Is the letter now tighter and more market-focused?

### Common failures
- Writer backstory that doesn't establish credibility ("I've been writing since I was 12...")
- Clever metaphors that don't clarify the pain or solution
- Tangents about personal philosophy that don't pay off for the reader
- Explaining WHY a technique works when the market only cares THAT it works

---

## Pass 6: Clarity & Pithiness Check

**No data file needed.** This is pure language editing.

### Checklist

- [ ] **Hemingway test**: Can you simplify this? Read each sentence and ask: "Could a 10th grader understand this immediately?"
- [ ] **Word count surgery**: Can 3 words replace 5? Can 1 word replace 3?
- [ ] **Cut throat-clearing**: "I wanted to write this letter because..." → Delete. Start with what matters.
- [ ] **Cut redundancies**: If two sentences say the same thing, keep the stronger one.
- [ ] **Shorten paragraphs**: Any paragraph over 4 sentences? Split it.
- [ ] **Sentence variety**: Are all sentences the same length? Mix short (3-5 words), medium (10-15), and occasional longer (20-25).
- [ ] **Active voice**: "Credit was stolen by the boss" → "The boss stole credit"
- [ ] **Remove "that"**: "I knew that it was wrong" → "I knew it was wrong" (unless needed for clarity)

### Common failures
- Long, winding sentences that lose the thread
- Academic vocabulary that makes the reader work
- Multiple clauses stacked in one sentence
- "And" as a connector when a period would be stronger

---

## Pass 7: Drama Check

**Check against**: `slang-dictionary.md` → pain language, `copy-brief.md` → Pain Point Bullets

### Checklist

- [ ] **Headline**: Does it grab? Does it make someone who needs this STOP what they're doing?
- [ ] **Opener**: Does it bag the attention? Does it make them want to keep reading?
- [ ] **Story**: Does it have emotional weight? Rock Bottom should feel uncomfortable. Transformation should feel earned.
- [ ] **Bullets**: Do they hit the gut? Would someone read 25+ without getting bored?
- [ ] **Close**: Does it maintain urgency? Does the deadline feel real?
- [ ] **Pain language**: Is the pain SPECIFIC and VIVID? "It hurt" → "It felt like a hot needle being pushed through his urethra"
- [ ] **Desire language**: Is the transformation SPECIFIC and VIVID? "You'll feel better" → "You'll walk into Monday morning staff meetings feeling calm instead of nauseous"

### Common failures
- Flat, generic pain descriptions
- Bullets that inform but don't provoke
- Story that tells instead of shows
- Close that loses urgency — "buy when you're ready" instead of "this offer closes Friday at midnight"

---

## Pass 8: Objection Sweep

**Check against**: `copy-brief.md` → Objection Inventory, `copy-brief.md` → Competitive Positioning Map

### Checklist

- [ ] Read as a SKEPTIC. Where would you push back?
- [ ] Is every claim backed by something? (Proof, specificity, story, or data)
- [ ] Does the guarantee address the #1 objection?
- [ ] Does the takeaway sound genuine or manufactured?
- [ ] Are the bonuses credible? Do they have verifiable value or made-up numbers?
- [ ] Is the price anchored? Does the reader understand why this is house odds?
- [ ] Is the deadline real? Does it have a reason beyond "I said so"?
- [ ] Would the market read this and think "scam" at any point?

### Common failures
- Guarantee sounds boilerplate ("30-day money-back" with no drama)
- Bonuses sound like padding ("$5,000 value!" for a PDF — no one believes this)
- Takeaway sounds like a tactic, not genuine exclusion
- Deadline has no reason to be real ("limited time" — why? What happens after?)

---

## Pass 9: Rhythm & Flow Check

**No data file needed.** This is about reading flow and momentum.

### Checklist

- [ ] **Sub-headline placement**: Every 15-20 bullets, is there a dramatic sub-headline to shatter monotony?
- [ ] **Bullet variety**: Are bullet types varied up? No same type 3 in a row.
- [ ] **Paragraph length**: Visual scan — any walls of text? Break them up.
- [ ] **Transition sentences**: Section-to-section transitions should feel natural, not announced.
- [ ] **Momentum**: Does the letter build? Faster pace in bullets, peak in close? Or does it stall mid-letter?
- [ ] **First 300 words**: The most critical real estate. Are they the strongest writing in the letter?
- [ ] **Last 200 words**: The close. Does it finish strong or trail off?

### Common failures
- Monotonous bullet rhythm — same length, same structure, same type
- Transition from story to bullets feels clumsy ("and now here are some bullets...")
- Close loses all momentum and turns into a feature list
- Opening is strong but middle sags

---

## Pass 10: Final Polish

**No data file needed.** The last check before delivery.

### Checklist

- [ ] **Spelling**: Every word. No assumptions.
- [ ] **Grammar**: Subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, pronoun consistency
- [ ] **Formatting**: Headers consistent, sub-headlines bold/centered, bullet markers consistent
- [ ] **Placeholders**: Any "[NAME]," "[PRODUCT]," "[PRICE]" left unfilled?
- [ ] **Data trace**: Can every major claim be traced back to a research file?
- [ ] **No missing sections**: Are all 5 parts present (even if some are minimal)?
- [ ] **Word count**: Total letter length noted
- [ ] **One final gut check**: "Would I buy this? Would the market buy this?"

## The 10-Pass Schedule

Adapted from Ben's 5-day schedule:

| Day | Passes | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 1** | Write first draft (all sections assembled) | "Write drunk" — no editing, just assembly |
| **Day 2** | Passes 1-4 (morning: 1-2, afternoon: 3-4) | Heavy structural editing |
| **Day 3** | Passes 5-7 (morning: 5-6, afternoon: 7) | Clarity, drama, market focus |
| **Day 4** | Passes 8-10 (morning: 8-9, afternoon: 10) | Objections, rhythm, polish |

In AI terms, each "day" is one editing session. Budget 3-4 sessions.

---

## The "Read It Out Loud" AI Equivalent

Ben reads his copy out loud 10 times. The AI equivalent:

1. **Each pass re-reads the ENTIRE letter** — start from the headline every time, even if the lens only applies to one section
2. **Each pass produces edits** — not just observations, actual changes
3. **Between passes, review market research** — Ben checks against his research between readings
4. **The 10th pass should find ALMOST NOTHING** — if pass 10 finds major issues, you need more passes
