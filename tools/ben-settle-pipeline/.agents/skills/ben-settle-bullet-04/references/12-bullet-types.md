# Ben Settle's 12 Bullet Types — Full Methodology

From *Copy Slacker* Chapter 6. Bullets are the most important "writing" part of sales copy. One bullet buried deep in an ad can make the entire sale. Gary Halbert's Entrepreneur magazine ad had a bullet that got people calling a full year after it ran. The headline is nothing more than your best bullet.

---

## Type 1: Curiosity

**Why it works**: Creates an itch they MUST scratch. Fascinating but incomplete. People eagerly give in to clickbait, buy products they don't need, and can't sleep until they know the answer.

**Construction**: Find something the market is interested in. Tell them something fascinating about it — but don't complete the thought. The incompleteness IS the hook.

**Classic examples**:
- "Seventeen lies all intelligent women always tell men!" — Gary Halbert
- "Cancelled check for fire insurance premium proves you're covered. Right? Wrong." — Mel Martin
- "Bills it's okay to pay late" — Mel Martin

**Data sources**: Slang terms, pain points, counterintuitive facts, enemy behaviors. Anything the market wants to know more about.

---

## Type 2: "Can't Be Done"

**Why it works**: A claim that's ALMOST unbelievable — right on the cusp of reality. Not impossible, just barely believable. Forces people to buy to find out if it's true.

**Construction**: Take a benefit your product delivers. Phrase it so it sounds almost too good to be true — but IS true.

**Classic examples**:
- "How to vacuum, dust, and clean your dresser drawer without removing its contents!" — Gene Schwartz
- "A secret, back door way of getting Google to reduce your pay-per-click fees... without even logging into your AdWords account." — Ben Settle

**Data sources**: Counterintuitive facts, secret fantasies, bizarre story-mining details.

---

## Type 3: Proof and Credibility

**Why it works**: Proof silences the inner skeptic. Every proof element you add is a link in a chain. "One fully believed claim is more persuasive than 1,000 half-believed claims."

**Construction**: Take a benefit. Attach a named authority, historical figure, scientific fact, or documented result to it. The proof makes the otherwise-hypey claim credible.

**Classic examples**:
- "A scientifically proven way (discovered by Isaac Newton) to hit another man with 'sledge hammer' like force."
- "The one word Socrates (perhaps the greatest master of persuasion who ever lived) used to 'sell' people on his ideas — even though they were dead set against him before!"

**Data sources**: Proof elements, historical story-mining (Cat 2), news events (Cat 5), numbers (Cat 6).

---

## Type 4: Contrast

**Why it works**: Contrasting ideas clamp down on psychology and don't let go. Humans need consistency — contrast disturbs that, creating an itch that can only be scratched by resolving the contradiction.

**Construction**: Take two ideas that don't belong together. Put them in the same bullet. The clash IS the hook.

**Classic examples**:
- "The incredible copywriting secret (used by both P.T. Barnum and Mother Theresa) that lets you sell to even your coldest prospects."
- "A popular 'vegetable' that can make you fat as a whale."
- "A sneaky way to sell more products with a WEAK sales pitch"

**Data sources**: Enemy vs. proof element, slang term vs. opposite meaning, competitor weakness vs. your gap.

---

## Type 5: Pain (Physical or Psychological)

**Why it works**: Pain sticks to the brain like nothing else. Memory trainers use pain imagery because the brain can't ignore it. People can't resist reading about pain they're going through or could go through.

**Construction**: Describe a specific, vivid, cringe-inducing consequence — either physical or psychological. Make them squirm.

**Classic examples**:
- "A secret 'bear hug' defense that's so ruthless, there's a good chance he'll be pissing blood immediately afterwards."
- "What many people do in their wills that could disinherit their children." — David Deutsch
- "Why some prostate supplements not only make your problems worse... but can also turn you into a girl!"

**Data sources**: Pain data (physical + emotional + psychological), anger triggers, keeps_awake_at_night.

---

## Type 6: Straight Benefit

**Why it works**: Sometimes you don't need a trick. When the benefit is urgent and specific, just state it. No bells, no whistles. The benefit IS the bullet.

**Construction**: State the benefit directly. Optionally add a second benefit to create the "double whammy" (John Carlton technique).

**Classic examples**:
- "How to hit the golf ball's sweet spot every time you swing the club. (Hit the ball in this exact spot and you almost can't help driving the ball farther and straighter down the fairway.)"
- "How to get your affiliate web pages featured on CNN, Google, Yahoo, and other high traffic sites 100% free."

**Data sources**: Desire anchors, secret fantasies, what_they_want_most.

---

## Type 7: Tell Them What NOT To Do

**Why it works**: Telling someone what not to do is inherently interesting — ESPECIALLY if they're doing it. "The #1 worst mistake" = they must know if they're making it.

**Construction**: Name a specific mistake. Add consequence. If possible, be hyper-specific ("four words," "the #1 worst").

**Classic examples**:
- "The #1 WORST mistake you can make with your wrist when swinging a golf club."
- "Four words that will destroy your chances of sponsoring new reps — even the ones who already want to sign up!"

**Data sources**: Mistakes/Failures inventory, objection inventory, failed solutions from story-mining Cat 4.

---

## Type 8: The Giveaway

**Why it works**: Giving away the ENTIRE secret in one bullet proves you're not a charlatan. The reader can test it themselves. It makes all your other, more outrageous bullets believable.

**Construction**: State the complete tip, fact, or secret. No tease. No withholding. Full disclosure. Use sparingly — once or twice per list.

**Classic examples**:
- "Nausea — settle a queasy stomach with a bowl of milk toast. Amazing!"
- "How to use simple cartoons to explode your sales... no matter what you sell!"

**Data sources**: Proof elements that can be fully disclosed, insider knowledge from story-mining Cat 8.

---

## Type 9: The 90% Solution

**Why it works**: Give 90% of the secret, withhold the crucial 10%. Like baiting a fishing hook — just enough to get the bite, not the whole can of worms. David Deutsch's method.

**Construction**: State the WHAT clearly. Withhold the WHY or HOW. The reader must buy to get the missing 10%.

**Classic examples**:
- "Why you should never chew gum if you are trying to lose weight!" — Ben Settle
- "The real reason why Prozac and Zoloft are so popular in this country! (Almost no one... not even doctors... understands the startling sexual implications.)" — Gary Halbert

**Data sources**: Slang terms with hidden depth, counterintuitive facts, pain data where the cause is surprising.

---

## Type 10: Dramatic Sub-Headline

**Why it works**: After 40-50 bullets, the list becomes monotonous. Breaking one bullet's last 6-8 words into a dramatic sub-headline shatters the pattern and re-engages attention.

**Construction**: Take a strong bullet. Turn its last 6-8 words into a bold, centered sub-headline. Continue the next bullet after it.

**Classic example**:
- "How to 'trick' copywriters who get paid hundreds of thousands of dollars per year...
  **INTO DOING ALL THE 'DIRTY WORK' FOR YOU!**"

**Use**: Every 15-20 bullets. 3-4 times per 100-bullet list.

---

## Type 11: Compressed Finale

**Why it works**: Like a fireworks grand finale — overwhelm with claims. After a lead-in bullet ("And a whole lot more, including..."), fire rapid short bullets in a paragraph block. The density creates an almost hypnotic effect.

**Construction**: Lead-in bullet → paragraph of 10-15 short bullets (one sentence each, separated by "..." → end with a dramatic sub-head.

**Classic example**: (See Settle's Affiliate Launch Copynomicon ad — paragraph bullets separated by "..." ending with dramatic sub-head)

**Use**: Once, at the end of the bullet list. The final overwhelming push.

---

## Type 12: Expansion Bullets

**Why it works**: Picking one bullet and expanding on it mid-list creates an editorial feel. "Let's take a second to unpack this." It's informational, builds credibility, and deepens the tease on a high-value bullet.

**Construction**: Pick a bullet. After it, write "Let's take a second to unpack this." Expand for 2-4 sentences. Reference specific pages/sections. End with a dramatic sub-head or transition back to bullets.

**Use**: Every 12-15 bullets. Creates the "loop effect" — expansion → bullets → expansion → bullets.

---

## Layout Tactics (Apply After All Batches)

1. **Beef up bookends** — Strongest bullet first. Strongest bullet last. Skimmers read these two first.
2. **Loop effect** — Expansion bullet every 12-15 bullets. Draw readers deeper.
3. **Vary up ammo** — No two adjacent bullets the same type. Keep them psychologically off balance.
4. **Eye relief** — Alternate bold/not bold. Simple 3-second design tip. Easier to read = more read = more buy.
