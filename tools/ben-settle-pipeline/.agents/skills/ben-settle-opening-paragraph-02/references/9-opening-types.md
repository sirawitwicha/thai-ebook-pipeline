# Ben Settle's 9 Persuasive Sales Letter Openers — Full Methodology

From *Copy Slacker* Chapter 4. The headline flags attention. The opening paragraph BAGS it — gets the prospect situated, settled in, and *ready and willing to read your ad*.

## The Opening Paragraph Mindset

Before writing any opener, internalize:

- **The job is to BAG attention** — not to sell, not to explain, not to list benefits. Get them settled in.
- **Curiosity is the engine** — most of these openers are curiosity-based. They create an itch.
- **It must not sound "salesy"** — Ken McCarthy: copy should sound like "a fascinating conversation you're having with someone about a shared interest." Not a pitch.
- **Adapt, don't copy** — the templates are shells. Fill them with YOUR market's language and YOUR personality. "Marketing Incest" happens when everyone copies the same thing word-for-word.
- **The bridge** — the opening paragraph connects the headline to the story. It transitions from "I have your attention" to "now settle in."

---

## Opener 1: "Let me tell you a story"

**Why it works**: Old faithful. Simple. Crystal clear. Everyone likes stories. No cleverness needed. No risk of sounding like advertising.

**Construction**: Start with "Let me tell you a story" (or a natural variation) and then begin telling a fascinating story from the research. The story can be about the product creator, a customer, a discovery, or the enemy.

**Settle's example**: *"Let me tell you a story."* (followed by the story — see next chapter)

**Adaptation formula**: `"Let me tell you a story about [the enemy / a customer who suffered / the discovery moment / the bizarre truth]."`

**When to use**: Always a safe choice. Especially good when the headline was emotionally charged — the story cools the emotional temperature and settles the reader in.

**Data sources**: The bar complaint monologue, the enemy description, the "Golf God" discovery moment from the research, a specific customer pain story from YouTube comments.

## Opener 2: If/Then

**Why it works**: The word "if" qualifies the claim and removes the "bull shit factor." Gary Halbert used this over and over. It says "you must do X to get Y benefit" — not "here's how to get Y benefit no matter what!" People instinctively know empty promises are lies. "If" makes it conditional and therefore credible.

**Construction**: `"If [condition the prospect meets], then [benefit / what this letter will show you]."`

**Settle's example**: *"If you would like to quickly double your sales using nothing but free Facebook posts, then this letter will show you how."*

**Adaptation formula**: `"If you [are experiencing the market's #1 pain / want the #1 desire], then [this letter will show you / you're about to discover] [specific outcome]."`

**Data sources**: The #1 urgent problem, the primary buyer description (the "if" condition describes THEM), the headline hook.

---

## Opener 3: If/Then (with proof)

**Why it works**: Settle's personal favorite. "If I had to pick one, I would use this every time." Same If/Then structure, but with proof elements injected. Proof is the rarest element in marketing — adding it here cements credibility and slips right past "sales defenses." Ken McCarthy's market breakdown: 50% never buy from direct response, 5% buy everything, but 45% are SKEPTICS who need proof. This opener targets them.

**Construction**: `"If you'd like to [benefit], then here's how [proof element — ancient/historical/expert/authoritative source] did it [timeframe/context]. And why you can do the same thing today."`

**Settle's example**: *"If you'd like to know a secret way to make yourself near invincible in a fight, then here's how ancient gladiators and samurai did it centuries ago. And why you can do the same thing today."*

**Adaptation formula**: `"If you'd like to [desire from research], then here's how [proof element — a specific authority, historical precedent, scientific finding, or documented case] [achieved/ discovered/proved] it. And why you can [benefit] today."`

**Data sources**: Proof elements, competitor intelligence (authority figures), slang dictionary (ancient/historical references like Machiavelli), desires and aspirations.

## Opener 4: Brutal and Blatant Honesty

**Why it works**: Being brutally honest — especially in an industry where nobody is — inspires trust faster than almost anything else. Admit the drawbacks first. The Shackleton ad: *"Men wanted for hazardous journey, small wages, bitter cold, safe return doubtful, honor and recognition in case of success."* Only one benefit. The rest are negatives. Got a tsunami of replies.

**Construction**: Lead with what you're NOT, what the product WON'T do, or the uncomfortable truth. Then pivot to what you CAN deliver.

**Settle's examples**:
- *"My name is Betty Adams and the first thing you should know about me is I am not a doctor. I am not an expert on nutrition either. I never went to college and I don't have a degree in anything. In fact the only thing I consider myself an expert about is..."*
- *"Yes, this IS a 'sales letter.'"*

**Adaptation formula**: `"The first thing you should know about me is I am not [credential the market expects]. I am not [another expected credential]. [Optional: I don't even...]. What I AM is [the one thing that actually matters to the market — proven by their own language]."`

**Data sources**: The objection inventory (address skepticism first), the market's insecurities, the competitor gap (what you're NOT — unlike Robert Greene, unlike HBR, unlike generic advice).

## Opener 5: Sensationalist

**Why it works**: Tabloid-style sensationalism hooks even people who claim they're "above" it. Gary Halbert: more copies of The National Enquirer sold every month than copies of the Bible sold throughout history. The sensationalist opener mingles "let me tell you a story" with a tabloid-like twist. You can almost imagine it on a tabloid cover.

**Construction**: `"Let me tell you a bizarre story about [someone/something in the market] who [sensational, surprising outcome related to the problem]."`

**Settle's example**: *"Let me tell you a bizarre story about a man who got arrested due to his prostate problems."*

**Adaptation formula**: `"Let me tell you a [bizarre/strange/unbelievable/disturbing] story about [a person/event/discovery in the niche] that [surprising, counterintuitive outcome]."`

**Data sources**: The tabloid quirk from the headline skill, the most surprising finding from YouTube comments, the "Golf God" discovery, a bizarre detail from the competitor analysis, the slang dictionary's most unusual term.

---

## Opener 6: Ask a Question

**Why it works**: Same principle as question headlines — neurologically impossible to ignore. A question about something the prospect is interested in lights up their neurology. It doesn't sound like advertising. It sounds like someone offering a new way to think. The late comedian Patrice O'Neal: giving someone an option for thinking differently almost guarantees they acknowledge what you're saying.

**Construction**: `"Did you know that [surprising fact that contradicts common belief in the market]?"` Then follow with implication.

**Settle's example**: *"Did you know that the long-term performance of baseball cards beats the pants off stock, T-Bills, and rare coins?"*

**Adaptation formula**: `"Did you know that [counterintuitive research finding] [beats/outperforms/contradicts] [what the market assumes]?"` OR `"[Provocative question the market asks themselves]?"`

**Data sources**: Section G (Do You Know curiosity bombs), competitive intelligence gaps, slang dictionary counterintuitive entries, the contrast between what the market believes and what the research shows.

---

## Opener 7: Do You Know?

**Why it works**: A variation on the question opener. Gary Bencivenga used this to sell a product about job interviews. The question MUST be something they really don't know — something that throws them for a loop and racks the shotgun. "Who doesn't give their dog a bone?" — it challenges a universal assumption.

**Construction**: `"Do you know [why/how/what] [shocking counterintuitive fact that challenges a universal assumption in the market]?"` Stack 2-3 of these. Then transition: these innocent mistakes can [serious consequence].

**Settle's example**: *"Do you know you should never give your dog a bone?"* (Followed by why they shouldn't let their dog chew on tennis balls or walk on driveways — innocent mistakes that can seriously injure, hurt, or kill their dog.)

**Adaptation formula**: `"Do you know [why you should never / what happens when / how] [common behavior the market assumes is fine]?"` Stack 2-3. Then: *"These innocent [mistakes/assumptions] can [serious consequence from pain data]."*

**Data sources**: Slang dictionary (common but wrong assumptions), daily frustrations (innocent mistakes they're making), the #1 urgent problem (what these mistakes lead to), pain data (the serious consequence).

---

## Opener 8: The Bite-Sized Open

**Why it works**: Most people have goldfish-like attention spans. Copywriters with short attention spans have an advantage — if it keeps OUR interest, it'll keep a prospect's. The bite-sized open is one line. Ultra-short. Immediately hooks and transitions.

**Construction**: A single, powerful sentence — often a fragment — that plants a vivid image, states a surprising position, or creates immediate intrigue. Then launch into the story.

**Settle's examples**:
- *"Picture this."*
- *"I was a total skeptic."* (Matt Furey's long-running control)

**Adaptation formula**: `"[2-4 words that plant a vivid mental scene / state a surprising identity / create instant curiosity]."`

**Data sources**: The bar complaint monologue (opening emotion), the dominant resident emotion, the primary buyer identity ("I was a total skeptic" — from the objection inventory), the headline hook (condensed to its essence).

---

## Opener 9: Would You Take Advice From?

**Why it works**: Ask if the prospect would take advice from someone they'd normally avoid. The more outrageous and dramatic, the better. It creates a contrast that's impossible to ignore. John Carlton's version got massive response. Warning: can pull in non-prospects if too sensational.

**Construction**: `"Would you take [niche] advice from [outrageous, unexpected source]? Sounds like a silly question, doesn't it? Well don't make up your mind too quickly. Because I've got a true story to tell you that [dramatic teaser]."`

**Settle's example**: (John Carlton) *"Would you take investment advice from a drug dealer? Sounds like a silly question doesn't it? Well don't make up your mind too quickly. Because I've got a true story to tell you that sounds like something right out of the Godfather."*

**Adaptation formula**: `"Would you take [niche] advice from [the enemy / someone the market despises / an unlikely source]? [Acknowledgment that this sounds absurd]. Because I've got a [true/bizarre/fascinating] story about [dramatic teaser from the research]."`

**Data sources**: The enemy (the outrageous source), the competitive positioning map (who the market doesn't trust), the proof elements (the unlikely expert), a dramatic story from YouTube comments or Reddit.

## Things to Remember

1. **The headline flags, the opener bags** — if you don't bag the attention here, the headline was wasted.

2. **Curiosity is the engine** — most of these templates create an itch the reader must scratch.

3. **Don't sound "salesy"** — fascinating conversation, not pitch.

4. **Adapt, don't copy** — "Marketing Incest" happens when everyone uses the exact same templates word-for-word. Fill them with YOUR market's language.

5. **Proof everywhere** — inject proof into every opener when possible. It's the rarest element.

6. **The transition matters** — every opener should end by setting up the story that follows.

7. **Be you** — the more these sound like your voice, the better they work.
