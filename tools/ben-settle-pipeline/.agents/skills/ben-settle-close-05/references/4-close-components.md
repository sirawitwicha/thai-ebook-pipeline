# Ben Settle's 4 Close Components — Complete Methodology

The close is where you lay out the guarantee, bonuses, takeaway, and PS — then ask them to buy. "If you did the first 4 parts right, did your research right, and are offering them something they want... then you've got them all dressed up wanting to go somewhere."

---

## Component 1: The Guarantee

### When to Use vs. Skip

**Use a guarantee when**:
- Selling to cold prospects (mass market)
- You need credibility and "safety" to buy
- The market has been burned before (check objection inventory)
- Competitors offer guarantees and you need to match/beat

**Skip the guarantee when**:
- Selling to your own warm list
- You want committed "Burn the Boats" customers
- The product is a service/consulting (scope issues)
- Ben's philosophy: "I don't like the kind of mindset it gives people when I bring them in as customers"

### Construction — The Drama Spectrum

#### Level 1: Conservative
Standard guarantee that neutralizes the top objection.

```
"If you're not satisfied for any reason within [timeframe], simply let me know and I'll refund every penny. No questions asked."
```

#### Level 2: Dramatic
Extended timeframe or enhanced terms that make the offer feel risk-free.

```
"Your grandkids can return it!" — John Carlton
```

```
"If this isn't the best [product type] you've ever [experienced], I'll refund your money, you can KEEP the product, and I'll pay you $100 cash just for wasting your time."
```

#### Level 3: Extreme (Balls-to-the-Wall)
Massively asymmetric guarantee — your risk, their reward.

```
"I'll TRIPLE your money back, you can keep the product, and I'll even pay $100 cash out of my pocket on the spot just for wasting your time!"
```

#### Level 4: Competitor Buyout
Offer to buy them your competitor's product if they're not satisfied.

```
"If this isn't the best [book/course/product] about [topic] you've ever [read/used], let me know and I'll send your money back including your shipping fees, you can keep the [product], and I'll buy you a copy of my closest competitor's [product] just for wasting your time!"
```

### How to Choose the Level

Pull from the objection inventory:
- "This sounds too good to be true" → Level 2-3 needed
- "I've been burned before" → Level 3-4 needed
- "What if it doesn't work for ME?" → Level 2 with extended timeframe
- "It's too expensive" → Level 2-3 (risk reversal makes price irrelevant)

Pull from competitive positioning:
- If competitors offer 30-day → offer lifetime
- If competitors offer money-back → offer double money-back
- If competitors offer nothing → Level 1 may be enough

### The "Burn the Boats" Alternative

For your own list or premium positioning:
- NO guarantee — signals confidence and filters for committed buyers
- "I want committed customers ready to take action. I have always found them to be the best customers."
- Losing a few sales from guarantee-seekers is worth the peace of mind

## Component 2: Bonuses

### Core Principle

**"Don't sell the product — sell the premium (the bonuses)."** The bonuses should be so valuable they have both more intrinsic value and more perceived value than the product itself. "People buy my offers just to get the bonuses."

### Bonus Selection Rules

1. **Touch strong emotional hot buttons** — pick bonuses that scratch a burning itch from the research
2. **Match the market's biggest pains and desires** — every bonus must trace to a specific research data point
3. **Verifiable retail value** — not "$X value" pulled from thin air. Use bonuses that are already selling elsewhere and have legitimate, proven retail value in the marketplace
4. **Name them in the market's language** — pull bonus names from the slang dictionary

### Bonus Types (with data sources)

| Bonus Type | Source in Research | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Quick Win** | Urgent crisis + what they want most | "The 5-Minute Monday Morning Power Move" |
| **Secret Knowledge** | Slang terms + insider language | "The Grey Rock Protocol: How to Become Invisible to Workplace Predators" |
| **Swiped File** | Competitor intelligence + proof elements | "12 Real Email Scripts That Stopped Gaslighters Cold" |
| **Tool/Checklist** | Pain data + failed solutions | "The 'Am I Being Manipulated?' 30-Second Checklist" |
| **Story Collection** | Story-mining data | "How 7 Real People Destroyed Their Office Tyrant — Without Losing Their Job" |
| **Insider Access** | Bar complaint monologue | "The Private 'Sunday Scaries' Elimination Audio" |
| **Done-For-You** | Pain data (what they don't want to do) | "5 Pre-Written Email Templates for Documenting Credit Theft" |

### The Descriptions

Each bonus gets:
1. **Name** using slang/market language (1 line)
2. **Hook** — why it matters, the pain it solves (1-2 sentences)
3. **Value anchor** — a real, verifiable reason for the stated value ("This report has sold for as much as $97")

### Hot Bonuses

If you have an especially hot bonus, give it:
- Its own short story
- Its own set of bullets
- Its own mini-headline

"Sell the bonus like it's the main product — because for many buyers, it IS."

## Component 3: The Takeaway

### What It Is

Discouraging certain people from buying — sometimes even NOT letting them buy. It's the opposite of needy selling.

### Why It Works

1. **Scarcity psychology**: "There's something about human psychology that drives us almost stark, raving mad when someone tells us we can't have something. When we are told we can't have it, we want it even more."

2. **Credibility**: "Putting up 'barriers' to a sale automatically boosts your credibility and believability."

3. **Filtering**: Keeps bad-fit customers out — reducing refunds, complaints, and support burden.

4. **Exclusivity**: "Makes what you sell more exclusive and prestigious."

### The Critical Rule

**The takeaway must be genuine, not a tactic.**

"You can't just throw out take-away lines and attitude and think anyone will take you seriously. Smart customers smell manipulative tactics like poop on a shoe."

"It's got to be matter-of-fact, and rooted in honesty, based on you really believing certain people should not have it."

### Construction — Who to Exclude

Pull genuine exclusion reasons from the research:

1. **Wrong mindset** — "If you're the type who likes to whine about 'information overload'..."
2. **Won't implement** — "If you're looking for a magic pill you can take once and wake up tomorrow with a corner office..."
3. **Wrong situation** — "If you work at a company of 5 people where everyone is family..."
4. **Financial risk** — "If buying this would put you in financial jeopardy..."
5. **Not the target** — "If you're already the CEO and have no one above you to navigate..."
6. **Wrong expectations** — "If you think you'll be able to use this to get rich by next Tuesday..."

### The Tone

- Matter-of-fact, not performative
- Honest, not attitudinal
- Protective of the reader, not condescending to them
- "I do it righteous... as an ethic."

### Where to Place It

- In the body of the close, before the CTA
- In a PS
- Both (reinforcement)

## Component 4: The Almighty PS

### Why It Matters

The PS is traditionally the second most-read part of an ad. Even if that's debatable online, it's valuable real estate.

### What Goes in a PS

#### 1. Testimonials (BEST use)
Testimonials in the PS don't interrupt narrative flow. They land AFTER the offer as social proof reinforcement.

```
P.S. Here's what [Name], a [role] at [company], wrote me last week:
"[verbatim testimonial]"
```

#### 2. Bonus Story
Tell a short story about a specific bonus — especially a hot one.

Example from Settle (for a Dan Kennedy/Ken McCarthy course):
> P.S. One more thing: A few years ago Dan Kennedy noticed many of the highest-pulling ads ever created use the exact same "clicks of the dial" — special phrases, ideas, offers, and themes that can super-charge any ad... Turns out there are 26 of them. And they're all contained in a secret list now worth a fortune to the few who possess it.

#### 3. Takeaway Reinforcement
Repeat the takeaway in the PS — it lands differently after they've seen the offer.

```
P.S. There is one "downside" to [product]: [genuine limitation]. Why? [honest reason]. That's why if you're [type of person who shouldn't buy]... Then Please Do NOT Order!
```

#### 4. Deadline Reinforcement
Hammer the urgency. "This offer really could vanish tomorrow. Maybe even tonight."

#### 5. Bullet Repurposing
Take a strong bullet from the bullet section and expand it into a PS mini-story.

### Multiple PS's

"Some ads have as many as 2, 3, or even 4 (and more) of them." As long as they have purpose, you're in their world, and you're keeping tension on, you can use as many as you want.

### The Dead ALWAYS Include a Deadline

"Every sales letter should have some kind of deadline in the close. It could be a date to get a bonus by, a lower price by, or just the threat of the current deal being closed at any time, for any reason."

Deadline types:
- **Date-certain**: "Offer ends Friday, May 30th at midnight"
- **Quantity-limited**: "Only 200 copies available at this price"
- **Launch window**: "This is the launch price — it goes up $50 after this week"
- **Bonus window**: "The [bonus name] is only included if you order by [date]"
- **At-will**: "I reserve the right to close this offer at any time, for any reason" (weakest, but better than nothing)

### What NOT to Put in a PS

- Nothing filler — every PS must have a clear purpose
- Don't repeat the offer mechanically — add new information or framing
- Don't put testimonials in the narrative flow — they go in sidebar or PS

## The Connector: Bullets → Close

Before the 4 components, you need a short, punchy connector. It bridges from the last bullet into the offer without a hard pivot.

### What Makes a Good Connector

- 1-3 sentences max
- Reads like a natural conclusion to the bullet section
- Doesn't announce itself ("And now, the offer!")
- References the primary hook implicitly
- Sets up what's coming without selling yet

### Example Patterns

"And all I ask is you hold off from disbelieving long enough to see the evidence for yourself. It will take just a few minutes..."

"The 12 patterns, the counter-tactics, the exact language manipulators use and how to reverse each one in real time — that's what you're about to see. It won't turn you into one of them. It will make sure they can't make you one of their targets."

"Everything I've just described — [bullet summary in 5 words] — is inside [product name]."

## Assembly Order

The close components can be arranged multiple ways. Here are the two most common:

### Configuration A: Direct Close
```
Connector → Offer (what you get) → Bonuses → Guarantee → Takeaway → Deadline → CTA → PS
```

### Configuration B: Takeaway-First Close
```
Connector → Takeaway (who shouldn't buy) → Offer → Bonuses → Guarantee → Deadline → CTA → PS
```

### Configuration C: Story PS Close
```
Connector → Offer → Bonuses → Guarantee → Deadline → CTA → PS #1 (testimonial) → PS #2 (bonus story) → PS #3 (deadline)
```

The choice depends on the market's psychology:
- Skeptical market → Configuration B (takeaway first disarms skepticism)
- Desperate market → Configuration A (get to the solution fast)
- Relationship-heavy market → Configuration C (PS stories deepen connection)

## The Anti-Salesy Test

After writing the close, read it aloud. Does it sound like:
- A friend telling you about something they genuinely think will help you?
- Or a salesman trying to "close" you?

The Copy Slacker close should feel like the former. You've done the work in steps 1-4. The close is just opening the door.
