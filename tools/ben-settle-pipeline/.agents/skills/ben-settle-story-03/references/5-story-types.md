# Ben Settle's 5 Storytelling Methods — Full Methodology

From *Copy Slacker* Chapter 5. Stories are the #1 skill in persuasive communication. Humans are hardwired for them. They sell even when you're not selling anything. Entertainers far out-earn teachers — stories let you do both at once.

## Why Stories Work

- **Top Gun never said "join the Navy."** Recruitment soared so high the military put recruiters IN the theaters. It sold millions of pairs of sunglasses — without showing the brand. Stories bypass sales resistance entirely.
- **Memory trainers use stories.** Your brain wants information delivered in stories. It "sees" them. Facts organized into a narrative are remembered.
- **Stories have been used for thousands of years** — for both good and evil. They've created religions, inspired atrocities, and sold billions in product.

---

## Story Type 1: Story About You

**Best for**: Origin stories. Discovery stories. Personal transformation. When the product creator has a compelling journey.

**Why it works**: Origin stories are one of Hollywood's most profitable genres. All ancient myths, fairy tales, legends, and religions kick off with an origin story. People are inherently interested in how something began.

**Construction**: Tell a story about something that happened to you (or the product creator) related to the product. Tie it into what you're selling. The story can be about: how you discovered the solution, how you struggled before finding it, or the moment everything changed.

**Key question to answer**: "What's my origin story?" Think hard about this. Write it down. It could be one of the single most profitable exercises you ever do.

**Settle's example** (MLM origin story):
> "Just a few short years ago... I couldn't sponsor a new distributor if my life depended on it! It's true. I was 29 years old at the time and, after five years in the business, I could count the number of people I had sponsored on one hand. No matter how hard I tried, I just couldn't make money in MLM — even though I worked it day and night... and even though I did everything my upline told me to do."

**When to use**: When you have a real personal story of struggle → discovery → transformation. When the product creator IS the proof.

## Story Type 2: Story About Someone Else

**Best for**: When you (or the product creator) don't have a personal story. When someone in the market has a more compelling story than you do.

**Why it works**: It gives you more options — you can pull stories from forums, blog comments, social media, biographies, news, anywhere. It grants "automatic and built-in kind of credibility or gravitas." And the stories are in the market's vernacular — describing the problem exactly how they talk to each other about it.

**Construction**: Retell someone's story as if you were sitting at a table with someone in your market, saying: "I heard about this guy who was having the same problem you're suffering with, here's what happened to him and here's how he took care of it..."

**Critical warning**: Do NOT copy and paste or plagiarize anybody's stories verbatim. Retell. Adapt. Make it yours. Never claim a fictional person used your real product — that's illegal in health and money niches.

**Settle's example** (prostate niche — 10-year control):
> "Let me tell you a story about a miserable wretch named ____. He suffered from an enlarged prostate and recurring prostatitis when he was only in his 20's..."

**Data sources in our pipeline**: YouTube comments (real people sharing real stories), Reddit threads, the bar complaint monologue (composite of real voices).

## Story Type 3: Dramatized Story About Someone Else (Parables)

**Best for**: When the market's pain is universal but no single person's story captures it all. When you need to compress multiple people's experiences into one narrative.

**Why it works**: Parables are extremely persuasive. Jesus did all his persuading in parables. They've created entire religions. They let you say a lot in fewer words. John Caples' "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano" is one of the most successful ads in history — a parable.

**Construction**: Create a fictionalized character who embodies the market's composite experience. Tell their story of suffering → discovery → transformation. The story dramatizes the PROBLEM (which is universal and true) without fabricating false product results.

**Critical legal warning**: Do NOT claim the fictional person used a real product to get real results (especially in health, money, or legal niches). Settle's approach: tell a story about a fictional "Barney Fife-like cop" entering a mob — to illustrate WHY the training is valuable — not claiming the fictional cop used the specific product. The story sells the PROBLEM and the NEED. The product details come after.

**John Caples' classic**: "They Laughed When I Sat Down At The Piano — But When I Started To Play!" — fictional character, universal experience, segues into the product.

## Story Type 4: The Historical Story

**Best for**: When the niche has historical figures, ancient practices, or documented precedents. When you want built-in gravitas.

**Why it works**: History has already been written. The stories are already created for you. "Reality is stranger than fiction" — historical stories give you automatic gravitas. They can make dull products sound extremely interesting and exciting.

**Construction**: Research the history of the topic, the product creator's lineage, the ancient origins of the method. Find the most dramatic, surprising, or "sexy" angle. Tell the historical story first, then connect it to the modern product.

**Settle's examples**:
- **Escape Combatives**: Dull topic (escapes from holds). Made thrilling by telling the story of the world's first "combat scientist" — a Shanghai cop in the early 1900s who was in 600+ documented fights, tested fighting styles, and taught US/UK soldiers how to kill Nazi stormtroopers with bare hands during WWII. Suddenly escapes were thrilling.
- **Gorilla Grappling**: Nobody in the market wanted to learn grappling. But "ancient samurai and gladiators and people who were thrown to lions would sometimes survive because they learned grappling" — instantly made the topic compelling.

**Key**: The historical detail doesn't have to be in the product. Settle found it through separate research. It just has to make the topic exciting AND be documented in recorded history.

**Data sources in our pipeline**: `proof_elements` with historical weight (Machiavelli, ancient texts), `slang_dictionary` pop culture references, competitor historical claims.

## Story Type 5: Meet The Guru

**Best for**: When you (or the product creator) don't have personal credibility yet. When you learned from someone with massive authority. When you want to borrow credibility without bragging.

**Why it works**: Takes all trace of bragging out. Built-in gravitas. It's not about you — it's about the guru you learned from. Perfect for newer business owners with no brand, no testimonials, no credibility. Robert Kiyosaki built his entire empire on "what my rich dad taught me" — positioning himself as the student passing on wisdom, not the self-proclaimed expert.

**Construction**: Frame the story around meeting/learning from the guru. You're the one who made the journey, weathered the challenges, and now you're passing the information on. The guru is the authority. You're the conduit.

**Settle's example** (golf market):
> "Recently, almost by accident, I met a guy with some special golf industry 'connections' who shared a secret with me that is like every golfer's fantasy. Frankly, it sounded too good to be true. And, I thought he was joking when he told me about it."

**Key**: You don't need to be the expert. You just need to have learned from one. The guru at the top of the mountain passes the knowledge to you. You pass it to the customer. This is the kung-fu movie trope — and it works.

**Data sources in our pipeline**: `competitive_intelligence.competitors[]` (authority figures in the niche — Robert Greene, Machiavelli, Jordan Peterson, HBR), `proof_elements` with named authorities.

## Universal Story Principles (Apply to All 5 Types)

1. **Tension is king.** Every paragraph should create a "what happens next?" itch. Heaven and Hell: show the prospect in hell (the problem), then show them ascending to heaven (the solution).

2. **"Short enough to get attention, long enough to cover the details."** Like a woman's skirt. Don't bloviate. Get to the bare essence. But include enough specific, vivid details to make it feel real.

3. **"Nothing bad ever happens to a writer."** — Matt Furey. Every bad experience is now story material. Settle's house was burglarized, lost $50K. Instead of curling up, he turned it into profitable stories.

4. **Stay in the market's world.** "If Jesus had started with 'here's how to be a good neighbor' it would have bored everyone." Instead, he told the parable of the Good Samaritan — a story completely relevant to the market of that time. Everyone had experienced or knew someone robbed on the Jericho road.

5. **Don't thicken the plot, thin it out.** Get to the point as fast as possible.
