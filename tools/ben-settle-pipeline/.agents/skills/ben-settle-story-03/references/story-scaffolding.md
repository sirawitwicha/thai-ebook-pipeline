# The 7-Part Story Scaffold — Section by Section

This is HOW you solve the AI memory problem. Stories need 400-575 words with emotional arc, specific details, and tension throughout. Writing them in one pass causes: forgotten details mid-story, truncated endings, lost emotional tension, or generic filler.

**The fix**: Write one section at a time. Each section is self-contained. You only need the story brief + the last paragraph of the previous section to write the next. No section exceeds 100 words.

**CRITICAL — Writing Order**: DO NOT write sections 1→2→3→4→5→6→7. The later sections (6-7) will get compressed as context fills up. Instead, write in THIS order:

```
STEP A: Write sections 6, then 7 (Evidence + Bridge — no continuity needed)
STEP B: Write sections 1→2→3→4→5 (Setup through Transformation — narrative chain)
STEP C: Re-read sections 5 and 6. Adjust section 5's last sentence if needed to flow into 6.
```

**Why this works**: Sections 6 and 7 don't depend on narrative continuity. They pull from the story brief's Proof and Bridge data — not from what happened to the protagonist in section 5. Writing them FIRST ensures they get full attention and quality. Then sections 1-5 build the emotional arc that naturally leads into the already-written evidence and bridge.

---

## The 7 Sections (Writing Order)

| Write Order | Section | Words | Job | Depends On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1st** | 6. The Evidence | 50-75 | It wasn't a fluke. Proof it works. Gravitas anchors. | Story brief only |
| **2nd** | 7. The Bridge | 25-50 | Transition into bullets/product. Emotional invitation. | Story brief only |
| **3rd** | 1. The Setup | 50-75 | Who, where, what they believed. The innocent "before." | Story brief + continuity |
| **4th** | 2. Problem Escalates | 75-100 | Specific suffering. Vivid details. Gets worse. | Last ¶ of Section 1 |
| **5th** | 3. Rock Bottom | 50-75 | The worst moment. The breaking point. | Last ¶ of Section 2 |
| **6th** | 4. The Discovery | 75-100 | The turning point. How they found the answer. | Last ¶ of Section 3 |
| **7th** | 5. The Transformation | 75-100 | What happened after. Results. Emotional reversal. | Last ¶ of Section 4 |
| Adjust | Flow check: 5→6 | — | Smooth the transition from transformation to evidence | Last ¶ of 5 + first ¶ of 6 |

**Total**: 400-575 words.

## Section 1: The Setup (50-75 words)

**Job**: Introduce the protagonist. Paint the "before" picture. Make them relatable.

**Data inputs**: Protagonist from story brief, their beliefs, their world before the problem.

**What to include**:
- Who this person is (one sentence)
- What they believed about how the world works
- A specific detail that makes them real (age, job, situation)
- End with: the first hint that something is wrong

**Example from prostate niche** (Settle):
> Let me tell you a story about a miserable wretch named ____. He suffered from an enlarged prostate and recurring prostatitis when he was only in his 20's. In his case, it felt like he had to pee (literally) every hour of the day and he couldn't even sit through a TV show without rushing to the toilet.

**Write this section now. Do NOT append yet — hold it. (Section 1 is written 3rd in the new order. Write sections 6 and 7 first.)**

---

## Section 2: Problem Escalates (75-100 words)

**Job**: Make it worse. Pile on the specific, vivid, sometimes uncomfortable details. The reader should think: "Oh god, that's exactly what I'm going through."

**Data inputs**: The Hell (pain data with exact quotes), The Enemy, Slang terms for the suffering.

**What to include**:
- 2-3 escalating details of the problem. Each one worse than the last.
- Specific physical/social/emotional consequences
- Use the market's EXACT language for the pain
- End with: "But you know what? That wasn't even the worst part."

**Example from prostate niche** (Settle):
> It was always an adventure just getting his stream started (usually it wasn't even a "stream" — just a burning trickle), then it would suddenly stop, and sometimes wouldn't restart at all. And, even when he did squeeze out more than a few drops, he felt like he never fully "emptied out" — dribbling for hours later in his underwear, with embarrassing wet spots in his pants he'd sometimes catch people pointing and giggling at.
>
> Even after cutting out liquids hours before going to bed, he'd still wake up 5, 6, even 7 times at night with an urgent need to pee... only to stand over the toilet for several minutes... with nothing but a trickle oozing out of his penis that hurt like hell!

**Write this section now. Append to file. Read the last paragraph of what you just wrote. Then move to Section 3.**

---

## Section 3: Rock Bottom (50-75 words)

**Job**: The worst moment. The emotional/psychological breaking point. The reader should feel the despair.

**Data inputs**: Rock Bottom Moment from story brief, `urgent_crisis`, the most emotional line from `bar_complaint_monologue`.

**What to include**:
- The one specific moment or realization where it couldn't get worse
- Emotional consequence (shame, isolation, hopelessness)
- What they tried that FAILED (builds credibility — they didn't just jump to the solution)
- End with: despair. "He had pretty much given up."

**Example from prostate niche** (Settle):
> But you know what? That wasn't even the worst part. You see, after a while, his prostate problems got so bad... he couldn't even have sex without being in agonizing pain! Why? Because during the rare times when he could get an erection at all (and even that was soft, and barely able to penetrate), he felt an intolerably sharp "pin prick" feeling when he ejaculated. Like I said, the man was a miserable wretch.

**Write this section now. Append to file. Then move to Section 4.**

---

## Section 4: The Discovery (75-100 words)

**Job**: The turning point. The "almost by accident" moment. The reader should think: "Wait, what did he find?"

**Data inputs**: The Discovery from story brief, proof elements, historical references if Type 4.

**What to include**:
- How they stumbled onto it (accidental, desperate, skeptical)
- What specifically they discovered or learned
- A detail that makes it credible (not "I found a magic pill" — specific: "a random article in a muscle magazine")
- End with: the first sign it might work. "He felt it working in as little as 3 days."

**Example from prostate niche** (Settle):
> Then one day, almost by accident... after reading a random article in a muscle magazine about the prostate gland, he gave himself a "prostate exam" by putting his finger up his rear and feeling it. The magazine said a man's prostate should be walnut sized and soft. But when ____ felt it, he realized... his prostate was as large (and hard) as a golf ball!
>
> It scared the daylights out of him. So, he sought out various doctors and experts... He was told to take medications that could cause a sudden drop in his blood pressure. Or random bouts of dizziness. Or "dry" ejaculation. Or massive headaches. Or antibiotics that ate up his stomach. Or humiliating impotence.

**Write this section now. Append to file. Then move to Section 5.**

---

## Section 5: The Transformation (75-100 words)

**Job**: The payoff. What happened after they applied the discovery. The "ascension to heaven."

**Data inputs**: The Transformation from story brief, `desired_feeling_on_arrival`, `secret_fantasies`.

**What to include**:
- What they did with the discovery
- Specific results (numbers, timelines, concrete changes)
- The emotional transformation (from despair → empowerment)
- The "I almost couldn't believe it" beat
- End with: "And best of all..." or "But it didn't just work for him."

**Example from prostate niche** (Settle):
> Anyway, when he took these herbs together using a unique process to formulate them... he felt it working in his prostate in as little as 3-Days! But, it didn't just work for him. He took his "formula" to friends and family who suffered from prostate problems, too. And, it helped many of them feel relief just as fast (and, in some cases, even faster) as it did for him. Needless to say, ______ knew he was on to something.

**Write this section now. Append to file. Then move to Section 6.**

---

## Section 6: The Evidence (50-75 words)

**Job**: Prove it wasn't a fluke. Anchor with credibility.

**Data inputs**: The Proof from story brief, historical references, competitor intelligence.

**What to include**:
- Others tried it and got the same result (social proof)
- A credibility anchor (historical, scientific, authoritative)
- "It wasn't just him" or "This has been documented..."
- End with: the product name or the guru reveal (if Type 5)

**Write this section now. Append to file. Then move to Section 7.**

---

## Section 7: The Bridge (25-50 words)

**Job**: Transition gracefully from story into the bullets/product section. Don't abrupt-cut. Don't hard-sell.

**What to include**:
- One sentence that connects the story to what comes next
- Natural segue: "And that's how ____ discovered..." or "What he found is what you're about to see..."
- Zero hype

**Example pattern**: "And all I ask is you hold off from disbelieving long enough to see the evidence for yourself. It will take just a few minutes..."

**Write this section now. The story is complete.**

---

## After All 7 Sections: Tension Check

Read the full story once. Fix any of these:

- **Flat opening**: Section 1 doesn't hook → rewrite the first sentence
- **Saggy middle**: Sections 2-3 aren't painful enough → add more specific, vivid details from pain data
- **Rushed discovery**: Section 4 feels unearned → add what they TRIED that failed first
- **Too generic**: Details could apply to anyone → replace with specific numbers, names, situations
- **Weak bridge**: Section 7 feels tacked on → smooth it into a natural transition

---

## Word Count Enforcement

After each section, count the words. If under range: add one more specific detail from the research. If over range: cut the weakest sentence. Do not skip this. The range exists because:
- Under 50 words → feels rushed, no emotional weight
- Over 100 words → reader loses the thread, story drags
