# Worked Example: Data → Headline Transformations

This reference shows EXACTLY how raw research data becomes headlines. Study these 15 transformations before generating any headlines. The pattern is always the same: **extract the market's exact language → apply a headline type → format**.

**IMPORTANT — This skill is NICHE-AGNOSTIC**: These examples use "dark psychology of office politics" research data to demonstrate the PATTERN. Your niche will have DIFFERENT slang, DIFFERENT pain, DIFFERENT enemies. That's the point. The extraction process is identical regardless of niche — the market's language changes, the method doesn't. When you work on a different niche, fill the extraction table with THAT niche's data. Apply the same headline type formulas. The headlines will be completely different because the raw material is completely different.

If you ever catch yourself inventing a headline that doesn't trace back to a specific piece of research data, stop. You're writing, not harvesting.

## Example 1: Harvesting from the Bar Complaint Monologue

**Source**: `copy-brief.md` → "The Bar Complaint Monologue"

**Raw data**:
> "Nobody told me there was a WHOLE OTHER GAME happening right in front of me that I wasn't even seeing."

**Why this is headline material**: This is the market's EXACT language describing their awakening moment. It contains the core insight of the entire niche: there's a hidden game they were never taught. This is a Knock-Knocks shotgun rack.

**Headline type**: Market-Oriented + Emotion (Combine & Conquer)

**Harvested headline**: *"They're Playing a Game You Were Never Taught."*

**Why it works**: It's 7 words. It names the exact experience every prospect has had. It creates instant recognition — "Yes, that's EXACTLY what it feels like." No claim made. No benefit stated. Just the market's reality reflected back at them. This is the shotgun rack.

## Example 2: Harvesting from the Slang Dictionary

**Source**: `slang-dictionary.md` → Entry #6 — "credit theft"

**Why it works**: Neurologically impossible to ignore — the prospect has asked themselves this exact question. Sean D'Souza's principle: you can't NOT notice a question that's interesting to you. This question IS their daily experience.

## Example 3: Harvesting an Objection

**Source**: `copy-brief.md` → "Objection Inventory"

**Raw data**:
> Objection: "I'm an introvert — I can't do political games."
> Neutralization: "Introverts have NATURAL advantages: observation skills, less need for social validation, comfort with strategic silence."

**Why this is headline material**: This is the market's #1 self-limiting belief. It appears over and over in comments: "this is some stuff of horrors for an introvert." Addressing it directly — and FLIPPING it — is the Objection Headline formula. Gary Bencivenga: put the objection right in their faces.

**Headline type**: Objection Headline

**Harvested headline**: *"I'm an Introvert — I Can't Do Political Games." You're Right. And That's Exactly Why This Works for You.*

**Why it works**: It disarms the skeptic by agreeing with them first. Then the twist — the introvert's "weakness" is actually their advantage. This is the "Golf God" pattern: name the objection honestly, then reframe. Even fire-breathing skeptics can't ignore it.

## Example 5: Proof + Promise

**Source**: `copy-brief.md` → "Proof Elements Inventory" + `competitive_intelligence.competitors`

**Raw data**:
> Proof: "Harvard Business Review: 'You Can't Sit Out Office Politics' — institutional validation"
> Competitor gap (HBR): "Sanitized, won't name the real dynamics, too corporate"

**Why this is headline material**: Gary Bencivenga's formula: join proof to your promise. HBR is a rare, hard-to-fake proof element. But HBR's weakness (sanitized, won't go dark) is our gap. Join the proof (HBR's authority) to our promise (the dark layer they left out).

**Headline type**: Proof Headline

**Harvested headline**: *"Harvard Business Review Says You Can't Sit Out Office Politics. Here Are the 7 Rules They Left Out — The Ones That Actually Work."*

**Why it works**: The proof (HBR) makes it credible. The gap ("they left out") creates curiosity. The promise ("the ones that actually work") addresses the skeptic who's read HBR and found it too corporate. One fully believed claim is worth 1,000 half-believed ones.

## Example 7: Harvesting a Contrast from Identity Data

**Source**: `structured-profile.json` → `identity_and_worldview.values` + `pain_and_fear.urgent_crisis`

**Raw data**:
> Values: "Competence — respect people who are actually good at their jobs"
> Crisis: "The collapse of the 'just do good work and you'll be fine' belief system"

**Why this is headline material**: There's a devastating contrast here: the market's highest value (competence) is exactly what's holding them back. This is the Contrast Headline formula: two contradictory ideas that create unbearable curiosity.

**Headline type**: Contrast Headline

**Harvested headline**: *"Why Being Too Good at Your Job Is Exactly What's Keeping You Stuck."*

**Why it works**: It attacks the prospect's identity — "I'm good at my job" — and reframes it as the PROBLEM. The contradiction creates an itch they MUST scratch. "Wait, what? How is being GOOD at my job a bad thing?" That itch forces them to read.

## Example 14: Mining the Enemy for Market-Oriented Headlines

**Source**: `copy-brief.md` → "The Enemy" + `structured-profile.json` → `desires_and_aspirations.enemies`

**Raw data**:
> "They thrive because YOU play fair and they don't."
> Enemy: "The credit thief — steals ideas and presents as their own"

**Why this is headline material**: The Enemy is the single most powerful character in your copy. Naming them in the headline is the market-oriented approach — no claim, no benefit, just the market's reality. It creates instant identification: "Yes, I KNOW that person."

**Headline type**: Market-Oriented Headline

**Harvested headline**: *"This Is Not for the Brown-Noser. Not for the Credit Thief. Not for the Office Politician. This Is for the Person Doing All the Actual Work While They Play Their Games."*

**Why it works**: It FILTERS. The wrong people won't read it. The right people will feel SEEN. This is the self-defense headline pattern: "Warning: Do not read this if you have moral objections to..." — it defines the audience by who they are NOT.

## The Harvesting Method: Step-by-Step

Follow this exact process for every headline you generate:

1. **Open the bar complaint monologue.** Read it aloud. Circle every phrase that makes you FEEL something.

2. **Scan the slang dictionary.** Look for entries with quotation marks — these are exact prospect words. Each one is potential headline material.

3. **Read the pain data.** `keeps_awake_at_night`, `anger_triggers`, `insecurities`, `daily_frustrations`. These ARE headlines in raw form — they're the market's complaints, formatted as data.

4. **Check the objection inventory.** Every objection IS a potential Objection Headline. The market's own skepticism, stated honestly, is the most credible thing you can say.

5. **Look for the "Golf God" moment.** Is there a phrase in the research — from a Reddit comment, YouTube rant, or the bar complaint — where the prospect literally said the headline? Harvest it. Don't rewrite it.

6. **Apply a headline type.** Not every phrase fits every type. Match the emotional weight. Anger → Emotion or Market-Oriented. Curiosity → Curiosity or Tabloid. Skepticism → Objection.

7. **Format, don't rewrite.** The best headlines preserve the market's exact words. Change as little as possible. If the market said "I'm done being taken advantage of," the headline IS "I'm Done Being Taken Advantage Of."

## Anti-Generic Checklist

Before finalizing ANY headline, verify:

- [ ] Does this headline contain a specific word or phrase FROM the research data (not generic copywriting language)?
- [ ] Could a competitor in a different niche write this same headline? If yes → too generic. Revise.
- [ ] Does this headline pass the Knock-Knocks test? Would it get the door opened or slammed?
- [ ] Would this headline make someone who doesn't belong in this market scroll past? (Good — it should filter.)
- [ ] If I cover up the headline source data, can I trace it back to a specific research finding? If not → you invented it.
