# Ben Settle's 11 Headline Types — Full Methodology

From *Copy Slacker* Chapter 3. Every headline type is driven by research, not creativity. "The answer is always in your market."

## The Headline Mindset

Before writing any headline, internalize these three frameworks:

### The Knock-Knocks Exercise
*"What can you say in 6 or 7 words when the person opens the door and, instead of slamming the door shut on you, they grab you by the arm, yank you inside, and demand you tell them more?"*

Pretend you're going door-to-door in the most hostile selling environment there is. Instead of someone racking a shotgun at you and telling you to get the hell off their lawn, they're practically KIDNAPPING you and making you tell them more.

### The Shotgun Rack
Dan Kennedy's protégé Doberman Dan was a cop for 15 years. A grinning thug broke into his house and stood over his bed. Dan reached under the bed, grabbed his shotgun, racked it. Instant, complete compliance. Dan said: *"Ben, there's nothing in the world more scary than a shotgun being racked in your face."*

Imagine your prospect walking through a park, lost in their thoughts, texting, completely oblivious — and then a shotgun racks in their ear. That's what your headline has to do. What is the shotgun "rack" for your market?

### Ken McCarthy's Buck-Naked-in-the-Woods
Imagine your prospect running through the woods buck naked, with a bloody steak on a necklace around his neck, dripping blood behind him, creating a trail being followed by a pack of snarling, starving wolves he can hear in the distance. What would you have to say to get THAT person's attention?

## The 11 Types

### 1. The Proof Headline

**Why it works**: "One fully believed claim is more persuasive than 1,000 half-believed claims." — Gene Schwartz. Proof is the rarest, most valuable element of persuasion. Gary Bencivenga, who saw over a billion dollars of direct mail testing, says: "Join proof to your promise in the headline."

**How to construct**: Take your biggest promise/benefit + attach a rare, hard-to-fake proof element to it.

**Classic example**: *"World's leading military combat instructor declassifies the secret supplement used by the Navy SEALS to battle pain, injury, body fat, and cardiovascular health problems."*

**Why that worked**: Anyone can claim to battle pain and body fat. But "world's leading military combat instructor" and "used by Navy SEALS" — those are proof elements competitors can't easily claim. Also note "declassifies" — an inherently dramatic word.

**Construction formula**: `[Credibility marker] + [dramatic verb: reveals/declassifies/exposes/documents] + [the promise/benefit]`

---

### 2. The Market-Oriented Headline

**Why it works**: It's all about THEM, in THEIR world, talking about their pain or insecurity. No claim needed. No benefit stated. Just the market's own reality, reflected back at them so accurately they almost HAVE TO start reading.

**How to construct**: Pull a dominant pain, fear, insecurity, or identity statement directly from your market research. Phrase it as a statement that makes the prospect think: "That's me. That's exactly how I feel."

**Classic example**: *"Warning: Do not read this if you have moral, ethical, or religious reasons against hurting or even killing someone who violently attacks you, your wife, or your kids."*

**Why that worked**: Taken right out of market research. Self-defense buyers (right-wing, Type A men protecting families) would read that and think: "This is FOR me." It also filtered out the wrong buyers. It promises nothing — just describes the market's worldview.

**Construction formula**: `[Attention word: Warning/To/For/If you] + [market's identity, pain, or belief in their language]`

---

### 3. The Emotion Headline

**Why it works**: Taps into what Clayton Makepeace calls the "dominant resident emotion" — the feeling always simmering below the surface. When you name that emotion, it's extremely difficult for them to ignore you.

**How to construct**: Identify the single strongest emotion from your research. Lead with it. Often starts with "Warning" or an emotion-charged word.

**Classic example**: *"Warning: Do not read this unless you are frustrated as hell with your golf swing!"*

**Why that worked**: There isn't a golfer in that market who ISN'T frustrated with their golf swing. It lit up their psychology like a Christmas tree. No claim made — just an emotion named.

**Construction formula**: `[Warning/Attention] + "unless you are [dominant emotion] with/about [market's problem]"`

---

### 4. The Blatant Offer Headline

**Why it works**: When people already know they want what you have, or the benefit is something they already crave, a direct offer can do the selling all by itself. The 40/40/20 rule: 40% list quality, 40% offer, 20% copy. Sometimes the offer IS the headline.

**How to construct**: State the most irresistible offer you can make, as directly as possible.

**Classic example**: *"Now You Can Try My Entire Swing Training System And Hot New Driver Video For Just $1"*

**Construction formula**: `[Now you can / Get / Try] + [the offer] + [for just / for only / at] + [irresistible price or term]`

**When to use**: Warm lists. Products prospects already know they want. When your offer is genuinely better than anything else available.

---

### 5. The Contrast Headline

**Why it works**: Contrasting ideas tap directly into neurology via curiosity. Gary Halbert taught his protégé Scott Haines that curiosity — not self-interest — is the most important appeal in advertising. People will buy expensive products just to scratch an itch in their brain.

**How to construct**: Take two ideas that seem contradictory but resolve when you learn more. The tension creates an itch they MUST scratch.

**Classic examples**:
- *"Beat the races by picking losers"*
- *"How to lose weight by eating more of these fatty foods"*

**Construction formula**: `[How to / Why / The] + [desirable outcome] + [by/while/without] + [counterintuitive method]`

---

### 6. The Tabloid Headline

**Why it works**: Tabloids get ridiculously high readership because of masterfully crafted headlines. The checkout counter is the most coveted real estate in a grocery store for a reason. A sensational "twist" changes the entire dynamic — what would be a boring claim becomes fascinating.

**How to construct**: Take a benefit claim and add a tabloid-style sensational detail — a unique quirk about the product, its creator, or an unusual result.

**Classic example**: *"Amazing secret discovered by one-legged golfer adds fifty yards to your drives, eliminates hooks and slices, and slashes up to ten strokes from your game almost overnight!"*

**Why that worked**: Remove "one-legged golfer" and it sounds like every other golf ad. That tabloid twist — "how the HELL is that possible?" — creates irresistible curiosity. It also has contrast built in.

**Construction formula**: `[Sensational word: Amazing/Shocking/Strange/Secret] + [discovered/uncovered/revealed] + [by/from] + [the tabloid quirk] + [benefit/result]`

**Source of the quirk**: Dig up something unique about the product creator (like a one-legged golfer), the origin story, or an unusual application during your research.

---

### 7. Curiosity Headline

**Why it works**: Creates an "implied mechanism" — something the reader MUST know. Gene Schwartz analyzed these and said they work because of the implied mechanism, not because they bowl you over with huge claims. They create an itch you have to scratch.

**How to construct**: Hint at valuable insider knowledge without revealing it. The reader can't resolve the curiosity without reading further.

**Classic examples** (Mel Martin fascinations):
- *"What never to eat on an airplane"*
- *"Bills it's okay to pay late"*
- *"How to outwit mugger in self service elevator"*
- *"How to get a refund on airline tickets when they say you can't"*

**Construction formula**: `[What/How/Which/Why] + [specific, narrow promise of insider knowledge] + [surprising context or constraint]`

---

### 8. The Straight Benefit Headline

**Why it works**: Direct. Simple. When the problem is urgent and the benefit is clear, no twists or tricks are needed.

**How to construct**: State the biggest, most urgent benefit as plainly as possible.

**Classic example**: *"Get Up to 12 Miles More Per Gallon From Your Car"*

**Why that worked**: Gas prices were a massive pain point. The benefit was specific, measurable, and addressed an urgent need.

**Construction formula**: `[Action verb: Get/How to/Learn/Discover] + [specific, measurable benefit]`

**When to use**: When the market's problem is URGENT. When the benefit is specific and measurable. When the awareness level is high (7-10).

---

### 9. The Question Headline

**Why it works**: Sean D'Souza says it is neurologically impossible for someone to ignore a question that's interesting to them. You can't NOT notice it. No hype. Doesn't look like "advertising" at a glance. Also: from a legal standpoint, you can make claims the government might not let you make by simply adding a question mark (check with a lawyer).

**How to construct**: Ask a question the prospect desperately wants answered. The question should come FROM the market, not from you.

**Classic examples**:
- Gene Schwartz: *"Which of These Health Problems Do You Want to Beat?"*
- Ben Settle: *"Has the Golf God Arrived or is This Just a Complete Scam?"*

**Construction formula**: `[Which/What/Why/How/Is/Are/Can/Will] + [question the market is already asking themselves] + [?]`

---

### 10. The Objection Headline

**Why it works**: Learned from Gary Bencivenga. Addresses skepticism right in the headline. Shows you have the balls to stand behind your product. Even "fire-breathing skeptics" can't ignore it. Builds belief and credibility from the first word.

**How to construct**: Name the market's biggest objection or skepticism — then either own it, reframe it, or resolve it. The skepticism should come directly from your research.

**Classic example**: *"Has the Golf God Arrived or is This Just a Complete Scam?"*

**Why that worked**: Settle's research in a 30,000-person Facebook group revealed that the market's exact objection was: "If you asked me this at my front door, I'd think it's a complete scam or that the Golf God has arrived." He didn't hide from it — he put it right in their faces. The response was "unheard of."

**Construction formula**: `[Name the objection honestly] + [and/but/or] + [the resolution or reframe]`

---

### 11. Combine & Conquer

**Why it works**: Mix any of the 10 types together. The more headlines you write, the more natural this becomes. Two types combined is often stronger than either alone.

**Classic examples**:
- *"When Doctors Have Headaches What Do They Do?"* — Proof (doctors) + Question
- *"An Open Letter to Readers of Black Belt Magazine Who Are Fed Up With the Hype Surrounding the Seemingly Endless String of Fighter of the Month Programs"* — Market-Oriented + Emotion
- *"How to Live to be 110 and Still Remember Where You Left Your Car Keys"* — Curiosity + Benefit + Emotion

**Construction formula**: Take any two types and merge them. Common powerful combos:
- Proof + Question
- Market-Oriented + Emotion
- Tabloid + Curiosity
- Contrast + Benefit
- Objection + Proof
- Emotion + Tabloid
- Market-Oriented + Proof + Curiosity (triple combo)

## Things to Remember

1. **The headline's only job is to get attention** — not to sell. It CAN sell, but that's not its job.

2. **Stay in the market's world** — as long as you do this, you cannot screw up your headlines.

3. **Sometimes you can break rules** — two of the most-mailed sales letters in history (Wall Street Journal's 17-year control and Gary Halbert's Coat of Arms letter) had NO headline at all. But master the rules before you break them.

4. **Study tabloid headlines** — Cosmo magazine covers and the National Enquirer are masterclasses in headline writing. The words they use, the way they structure, how they create "pop."

5. **Study the greats** — Mel Martin, Gene Schwartz, Gary Halbert, Gary Bencivenga, Clayton Makepeace, Jim Rutz, Dan Kennedy, John Carlton, and others. Their ads are easily found online.

6. **The answer is always in your market** — if you grow bored of hearing this, too bad. It's what separates you from the bleating herd of copywriters nattering on about dorky ninja persuasion tricks.
