And whether headline or bullet point...

**Contrast Clamps Down Hard**
**On A Person's Psychology**
**And Doesn't Let Go!**

It doesn't matter if it's contrasting words, contrasting colors, contrasting patterns, contrasting ideas, or anything else that contrasts. It gets attention and is psychologically disturbing in some ways, which is one of the goals of a good ad:

**To Psychologically**
**Disturb People!**

It doesn't necessarily mean disturb them like you're scaring them (although some ads should definitely be doing that) but we're talking about disturbing them with things they have to resolve mentally. One of which is to make things make sense in their lives. In this case, they have to get your product just to resolve some kind of contrast, because it's bothering them and we humans have this need for consistency and for things to make sense. Stanford psychologist and Author Robert Cialdini's book *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* talks a lot about this, and it's well worth reading.

Here are a few examples:

-   The incredible copywriting secret (used by both P.T. Barnum and Mother Theresa) that lets you sell to even your coldest, most “sales resistant” prospects.
-   A sneaky way to sell more products with a WEAK sales pitch
-   A popular “vegetable” that can make you fat as a whale.

Lots of contrast in those bullets:

PT Barnum & Mother Theresa, sell more with a weak pitch, a vegetable that makes you fat... all contrasting ideas that get attention and influence people to want to know the answer.
