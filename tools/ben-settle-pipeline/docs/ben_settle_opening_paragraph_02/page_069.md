**Let me tell you a bizarre story about a man who got arrested due to his prostate problems.**

You can almost imagine that on a tabloid cover.

**Sales Letter Lead #6:**
**Ask a Question**

We covered this with headlines, too. And just like with headlines, a question about something your prospect is interested in all but lights up his neurology and flags attention. Which is why you can ask a question in the opening paragraph and get focused readership as a result.

Again, it doesn't come off as hype.

It doesn't sound like you're trying to sell them anything.

It's just a tempting, candy-like question.

Here is an opening paragraph I found in a book Gary Halbert compiled (I have no idea where to get it now, I found mine on eBay over a decade ago) that does this brilliantly:

**“Did you know that the long-term performance of baseball cards beats the pants off stock, T-Bills, and rare coins?”**

To an investor that's like catnip.

It asks a question related to something they are already interested in, and gives them an option for thinking differently. Giving someone options for thinking differently (something I learned from the late comedian Patrice O'Neal, who used this to get attention in his comedy acts) almost guarantees someone at least acknowledges you're saying something. They may not agree with it, but they can't ignore it. Their brains won't allow it. It is saying you have something new and unique to say. Something they would not ordinarily take seriously, probably. All of which is why is why a simple question in your opening paragraph can make your advertising automatically more credible, more believable, more interesting, and less “advertising” sounding.
