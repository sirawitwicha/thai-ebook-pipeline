But for now, realize contrast is powerful because of that. In fact, the
company Boardroom, Inc used to employ a brilliant copywriter many years ago
named Mel Martin. He was the man who created the “fascination” — which are
headlines and bullets that are just dripping with drama and curiosity — many
of which use a lot of contrast. He was the king of curiosity, and I believe a lot of
his success was because of his ability to maximize curiosity in his headlines.

Here is an example of a contrast headline:

**“Beat the races by picking losers”**

If you're into horse racing you would almost have to read it because that
contrast would bother you psychologically.

Here's another one:

**“How to lose weight by eating more of these fatty foods”**

Both of these are old school headlines, and I'm not exactly sure who
wrote them. But people who are thinking fat is bad are going to have a lot of
trouble dismissing this second one outright — especially back when it was
written. This goes back to the market's fantasy. Someone who wants to lose
weight but really loves their junk food is going to eat this headline up. And
they'll do it because of the contrasting ideas.

Tabloids get ridiculously high readership because of their masterfully

counter — they “get” people while waiting in line. The checkout counter is the
most coveted real estate in a grocery store because of this. I once sold a
product (you can read the ad in the swipe file at the end of this book) for a
product on how to get your product into Walmart, and the expert at how to do
that was saying if you can get your products by the register...

**You're Going To Automatically**

**Just Being There.**

That's why you see tabloids at the checkout — their headlines.
