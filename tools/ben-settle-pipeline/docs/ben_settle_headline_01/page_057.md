And you can tap into that phenomenon in your headlines, too. What you
do is add a sensational “twist” to your claim. One of my favorite examples is
from the great John Carlton he said ran for eight years in stuffy golf magazines
where many would think people wouldn’t want to read this sort of thing.

The headline was:

**“Amazing secret discovered by one-legged golfer adds fifty yards to your**
**drives, eliminates hooks and slices, and slashes up to ten strokes from**
**your game almost overnight!”**

**Think about this:**

If you remove the one-legged golfer part, it sounds like your typical ad.
Most would just roll their eyes at it as another claim they’ve heard many times
before. But that tabloid sensational twist changes the dynamic of the entire
headline. It also has that contrast going for it, “How the hell is that even
possible?” That’s what tabloid headlines do, which is why you should always be
studying tabloid headlines.

The National Enquirer has especially great headlines, and you can use
that sort of sensationalism in your headlines. All you have to do is — during
your research — dig up a unique, “tabloid-like” quirk about your product or
about whoever created your product (like a one-legged golfer).
