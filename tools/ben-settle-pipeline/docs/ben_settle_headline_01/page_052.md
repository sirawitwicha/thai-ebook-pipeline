**One Fully Believed Claim**
**Is More Persuasive**
**Than 1,000 Half Believed Claims**
**In Sales Copy.**

Thus, the power of putting proof in your headlines.

This is not the only way to do a headline, but it’s a reliably effective way
to do it. And what you do is like Gary Bencivenga says: **join proof to your**
**promise in the headline**. If you have an ad right now with a lame headline
now, test doing this and watch what happens. I’d be amazed if it didn’t improve
your response. I’m not saying it will every time, and you never know until you
test. But doing so can give yourself a huge advantage.

Here’s an example I used to sell supplements:

**“World’s leading military combat instructor declassifies the secret**
**supplement used by the Navy SEALS to battle pain, injury, body fat, and**
**cardiovascular health problems.”**

Let’s analyze this for a second.

If this headline just said “How to battle pain, injury, body fat, and
cardiovascular problems” it would be a boring headline. It would be a headline
anyone else could say. But look at the proof — “world’s leading military combat
instructor,” which the client was at the time; and it was used by the Navy
SEALS. Anyone could make the same claims as far as pain, injury, and body
fat. But none of his competition could claim (or easily claim) those two proof
elements. So now the idea of battling pain, injury, body fat, and cardiovascular
health problems has a lot more believability.

There is also the the word “declassifies” — which is inherently dramatic,
adding even more power to the headline, which makes it even harder to ignore.

Proof is what’s going to help people reading your ad believe you. If you
have the right offer, they want to believe you. But there is that little voice in
their heads telling them it must be a scam, it must be an exaggeration, it must
be bull. By putting proof in your headlines — right up front and center, as the
first thing they see — you blunt that voice. And, in many cases, enough to get
them to the next step (the opening paragraph) in the Copy Slacker formula.
