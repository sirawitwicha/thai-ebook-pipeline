You probably have heard the term, “the plot thickens.” But when it
comes to telling stories in sales copy, I say don’t thicken the plot, thin it out. In
other words, get to the point as fast as possible, in as pithy a way as you can,
and don’t drone on forever, bogging your reader down in details they don’t
need.

My view on this is, a story in sales copy should be like a woman’s skirt:

**Short Enough To Get Attention,**
**But Long Enough To Cover The Details!**

You do not want to sit there and bloviate, and you don’t want to ramble
on. Get it down to the story’s absolute bare essence to make your point, and
then move on into the next part which is the bullets.

Another thing:

**Always remember that tension is king.**

The best stories are full of drama and tension. That’s what makes them
good and stick inside your prospect’s psychology. Ideally, they don’t even think
about your copy as “advertising” — but as an exciting story they can’t put
down. You always want them to be thinking, “oh man what’s going to happen
next?” “What happened to that guy?” “How the hell did he get out of that
situation???” So you ideally want to make things like a “heaven and hell” effect.
You want to show them how they’re in hell now (i.e. they have a terrible
problem they don’t know what to do about, or they want something they can’t
have...) and then you show them how to ascend into heaven (i.e. get rid of the
problem vexing them or get the thing they want so badly).

Here’s something I heard the great Matt Furey say:

**Nothing bad ever happens to a writer.**

Everything that happens to you — especially bad things — are now
stories that could be used in sales letters or emails, on your blog posts, in your
videos, in your podcasts, whatever you’re doing. Everything that happens to
you that sucks now potentially becomes something you can use to sell with.
