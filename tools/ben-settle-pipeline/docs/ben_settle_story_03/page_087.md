I am a huge John Caples fan. His books, research, testing, and teachings
have been invaluable to me and just about every other copywriter I know who's
studied his material. He was an absolute genius at copywriting. And one of his
most successful ads (from 1927) is a parable selling a piano course. It had the
famous (and often swiped) headline:

**They Laughed When I Sat Down**
**At The Piano —**
**But When I Started To Play!~**

The ad is about a guy at a party who sits down to play the piano.
Everyone there is snickering and mocking him, thinking he's about to make a
jack ass out of himself. But, he ends up blowing everyone away with his skills.
Then the ad segues into the guy telling everyone he learned how to play via the
product being sold.

Sounds innocent, no?

It is and isn't.

By that I mean, telling a fiction story about a fictional person who used
your real life product or service to get a real result can get you in a lot of
trouble — especially in markets related to making money and health. As I
mentioned before, I'm not a lawyer nor do I play one in a copywriting book. But
I sell a $97 per month newsletter about email marketing called “Email Players.”
And if I told a story in the ad about a fictional person who made a million
dollars from his emails using my newsletter, but the guy doesn't really exist, I'd
get in a lot of trouble (and rightfully so). Same with selling, for example, a
health supplement. If you sell a health supplement and tell a story about how
someone who doesn't exist used your very real supplement to get rid of her
acne, you'd be setting yourself up for a world of hurt, potentially. There's a lot
of red tape and laws put there to protect consumers, and you've got to be
aware of them especially when you're telling parables. You don't want to say
someone who doesn't exist used a real product to get real results. It's
misleading. Yes, I know they do this on TV commercials all the time with
detergents etc. But we're talking about direct response marketing. At the very
least — and this is a good idea for all your copy — have a lawyer review your
ads. I've done this and it is astonishing how a lot of things people think are
okay can get you in a lot of potential trouble. Plus, I have found that by strictly
