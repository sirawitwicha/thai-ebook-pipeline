**More ideas for editing:**

Over the years, I have developed a systematic way of editing that works
really well for when you get that first, readable draft. It may still be riddled with
typos and have lots of rough spots, and still be too clunky and long-winded,
but it's at least readable by you and is starting to take the shape it'll ultimately
end up as. Sort of like a sketch where you've got it past the stick figure stage,
and it's there, but has to be refined. You see the form and the shape, but it's
still rough. At that point let it sit for as long as you can — ideally at least a day
or two. This isn't mandatory by any means. But realize the longer you can get
away from it the better, usually. Some copywriters let their copy sit for six
weeks when they can swing it. But either way, you want to get away from it for
as long as possible.

**At this point, what you want to do is...**

**Read It Out Loud**
**10 Times!**

I won't sugar coat this.

At this point, you're in for a long, tedious, and frankly boring experience.
But it's absolutely necessary if you want copy so tight you can bounce a
quarter off it and so crisp and clear only someone who is illiterate won't be able
to understand and follow it.

**Why read it 10 times?**

**Why not just 3 or 5 or 7 times?**

All I will say is, I've done it less than 10 times, and the copy always
suffered when I did. There is something about 10 times that just makes it all
work. Each time you read it — and the first 2 or 3 times will take the longest —
you will catch all kinds of gaps in your arguments. You'll find better ways of
wording things. You'll probably add new ideas and arguments and find ways to
make your sub-headlines and bullets more exciting. You'll discover all kinds of
