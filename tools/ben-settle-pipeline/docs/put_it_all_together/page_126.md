For example:

I will go through my notes and research one at a time and, anything that
looks like it could make for a great headline idea, I'll put under the headline
section. Anything that seems like it'd make a good opening paragraph — even if
it's just a few words, an idea, or a concept — I'll put under the opening
paragraph section. Any story ideas, go under the story section. And the same
with bullets and the close.

When you do this, it won't look pretty or even coherent.

And that's okay, it's not supposed to.

What you're doing is organizing your ideas and notes, and putting them
under the appropriate sections. And don't be shocked if you have notes and
research ideas that could go under multiple sections. That's fine. Copy and
paste it over.

After you've done this you're going to do what I call:

**The Money**
**"Message Massage"**

This is where you "massage" everything together.

What I do is I take one section at a time and, whatever interests me or
stands out, or excites me or inspires me, start playing around with it. If I am
looking at the headline section, for instance, and I see a note or an idea, I will
start writing it out as various headlines from the headline chapter of this book.
Maybe it's an emotion, or a piece of proof, or an interesting claim... whatever it
is, I will play with it. Reword it. Add and subtract words. Or just delete it
altogether if it's not going anywhere. By the way, to be safe, make sure you
keep a copy of your original "chaos" file somewhere, in case you delete
something you later realize you want to use after all.

Now, this phase can be taxing to the mind.

And it's very easy to get so caught up in the process (which is what you
want) you lose all track of time, and run the risk of burning out or losing your
creative edge. So what I do — and recommend you do — is use the Gene
Schwartz kitchen timer trick. He wrote a tremendous amount of copy and
